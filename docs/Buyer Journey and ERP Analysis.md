# **Hospitality Buyer Journey & ERP Landscape Analysis**

**Analysis Date:** March 26, 2026 **Dataset:** 204 hospitality deals (96 closed-won, 108 closed-lost) | ~878 call transcripts

---

## **Part 1: The Actual Buyer Journey (What Really Happens, Call by Call)**

Your hypothesis about messaging misalignment is supported by the data, but the problem is more specific than "wrong messaging." The issue is that **your sales motion assumes a feature-led, demo-first journey, but hospitality buyers follow a trust-driven, pain-first journey where the economic buyer's presence on the first call determines everything that follows.** Here's what the transcripts actually show.

### **The Winning Journey (Observed in 80+ of 96 Closed-Won Deals)**

**Stage 1: Warm Entry + Pain Validation (Call 1, Days 1–7)**

The first call in won deals is almost never a cold intro. The overwhelming majority of closed-won deals started through a warm channel — a TrueClub partnership referral, a CMAA conference connection, a peer recommendation from another club's Controller, or an inbound from a known pain event. Rio Verde Country Club came through the TrueClub program. Carmel Country Club's Stephanie Anderson came through CMAA. Denver Country Club's Courtney Hunt met Hunter Gibson at a CMAA conference booth. Equator Coffees came through an operational network referral.

What happens on this call: The AE meets 1–2 people (usually the Controller or CFO, sometimes with the GM). The conversation is pain-led — the prospect describes their current AP workflow, and the AE asks about invoice volume, payment methods, systems, and where things break. The critical signal on this call is **who's in the room and whether they quantify their pain in dollars and time.** In won deals, the economic buyer (CFO, Controller, or GM/COO) attended Call 1 in approximately 80% of cases. They didn't ask "how does it work" — they asked "how much will it save me" and "how fast can I move."

The prospect's mindset at this stage: "I know I have a problem, and I'm checking whether you understand my world." They're evaluating whether Ottimate speaks their language — Jonas, NorthStar, club accounting, multi-entity GL coding, check fraud, department head approvals — not evaluating features yet.

At Rio Verde, Anthony Paino (GM/COO) was on Call 1 and immediately asked about rebate economics. At Carmel, Stephanie Anderson (CFO) opened by describing a $850,000 check stolen from the post office and her frustration flipping between Beanworks and her banking system. At Denver, Courtney Hunt (Controller) admitted she was *"still trying to close February"* and was *"two weeks behind my own schedule."* These are buyers who already know they need to change — they're validating whether Ottimate is the right partner.

**What triggers the next call:** The AE proposes a demo with the broader team or moves directly to pricing. In won deals, the AE specifically asks who else should attend and gets names. The call is scheduled before the current call ends.

---

**Stage 2: Team Demo + "Aha Moment" (Call 2, Days 7–15)**

This is the most important call in the entire journey. In won deals, the demo call expands from 2 people to 4–6. The AE brings a Solutions Engineer or product specialist. The prospect brings their AP staff, sometimes department heads, sometimes IT.

What happens: The SE walks through Ottimate using scenarios that mirror the prospect's actual workflow. At Boca Woods, Sergio Rubio demonstrated multi-entity payment routing across the club and POA entities. At Concord Hospitality, Tyler Slaughenhaupt walked through intercompany allocation and property-level GL mapping across 100+ hotels. At Equator Coffees, Morgan Macko showed batch invoice processing for 400–500 invoices per week with multi-location GL coding that Bill.com couldn't handle.

The "aha moment" is visible in the transcripts. At Country Club of Peoria, Mary Mercer (Controller) realized the system could eliminate the 100% manual data entry she'd been doing alone — *"We need to enter them all manually. I do all that."* At Denver, Courtney Hunt heard that Admiral's Cove went from closing on the 14th–15th to the 5th–6th with Ottimate, and immediately said *"I would normally have been around fourteenth or fifteenth. Yeah, I am two weeks behind of my own schedule right now."* At Discovery Land, Tom Cullen (Director of Financial Accounting) opened with the joke *"I'm so far behind I think I'm winning"* — and then got serious when he saw how multi-club consolidation could work.

**The prospect's mindset shifts here from "do you understand my world?" to "could this actually work for us?"**

In lost deals, this is where the journey diverges. At Elite Casino Resorts, the call lasted 115 minutes but was tactical rather than strategic — Nicole Wright (Finance Director) asked *"Can you do all or nothing?"* rather than describing pain. At Scarlett Hotel Group, Eric (Corporate Director of Finance) questioned per-invoice pricing but admitted *"I've never actually sat and done the math on what is it taking right now."* At Metropolitan Club SF, the demo went well but the real buyer (the board) never saw it.

**What triggers the next call:** In won deals, the prospect asks about pricing, implementation timeline, or integration specifics. These are buying signals — they're mentally moving from "could this work?" to "what would it take?" The AE proposes a scoping/pricing call and schedules it on the spot.

---

**Stage 3: Scoping + Technical Validation (Call 3, Days 14–25)**

This is where the SE earns the deal. The conversation shifts from "what does Ottimate do" to "how will Ottimate work in our specific environment." Topics include: ERP integration specifics (Jonas API, NorthStar file upload, Sage Intacct sync, Great Plains data bridge), approval workflow design (who approves what, at what dollar threshold, with what fallback for absent department heads during busy season), payment method setup (check vs. ACH vs. virtual card, bank account routing per entity), and implementation timeline.

In won deals, every technical question raised in this call got answered — either live or within 48 hours. At Carmel Country Club, Stephanie Anderson walked Tyler through her integration landscape (Encore, Clear Sky, Jonas POS) and Tyler addressed each one with specific examples from comparable clubs. At Denver, Courtney asked about split-coding across departments, multi-GL invoices, and mobile approval for GMs on the course — Katie answered each with feature demonstrations. At Concord Hospitality, Andrew Switala (Sr. Director of Network Administration) joined specifically to validate the technical architecture, and Tyler walked him through the DMS replacement and property-level deployment model.

**The prospect's mindset here: "I believe it could work. Now prove it won't break."**

In lost deals, this is where unresolved requirements become fatal. Elite Casino Resorts needed PO matching with their SWS procurement system — Patrick (Accounting Director) said *"Our SWS already does that,"* minimizing Ottimate's differentiation. Fairfield Glade needed NorthStar integration and had Beanworks paid through year-end — the incumbent created a natural delay. SeoulSpice ran NetSuite and the native AP module was deemed sufficient for their low invoice volume.

**What triggers the next call:** The AE proposes pricing. In won deals, the CFO is already engaged or explicitly scheduled for the pricing call. In lost deals, the AE presents pricing to the controller and hopes they'll carry it upward.

---

**Stage 4: Pricing + ROI Justification (Call 4, Days 20–35)**

This is the call where deals are won or lost — not because of price, but because of **who hears the price and what context surrounds it.**

In every closed-won deal, the CFO or economic equivalent was on the pricing call. At Rio Verde, Anthony Paino (GM/COO) heard the pricing — $664/month after TrueClub discount plus $200–$1,500/month in projected virtual card rebates — and said *"So basically, we're going to make money by doing this."* Five days later, the deal was closed. At Sarasota National, Darrell Wilde (GM/COO) said *"Looks cost neutral to me."* At Denver, Courtney Hunt said *"That looks great. It's actually much lower than I was kind of, I wasn't really sure what to expect."* At Avalon Yacht Club, Peggy Tregear (Controller) calculated: *"I estimated between, what I am currently spending about 12,000. So that is a savings on top of it right there that I can present to the board."*

The winning pattern on pricing calls: the AE presents three numbers together — the subscription cost, the cashback/rebate opportunity, and the net effective cost. At Rio Verde, Eric showed $664/month subscription against $200–$1,500/month in rebates, making the system cash-flow positive. At Carmel, Tyler presented custom incentive pricing at 85 basis points and $1.10/transaction, benchmarked against Beanworks' existing cost.

In lost deals, pricing was either never presented or presented without this context. At Elite Casino, no pricing call was ever scheduled — Nicole asked *"Where's the value?"* but no ROI model was built. At Jackmont Hospitality, the administrator said directly: *"The $1.31 cost right here isn't, from my perspective, isn't sufficient savings for our current operations"* and *"I don't see a true cost savings for us — it looks like a wash."* At Metropolitan Club SF, Jorge (Controller) rationalized the math — *"When you break it down by month, it's only a 1,000 dollars"* — but couldn't get budget approval from a conservative new board.

**The prospect's mindset: "Is this worth the money, the disruption, and my political capital to push through?"**

**What triggers the next call:** In won deals, the prospect asks about contract terms, implementation start dates, or who their CSM will be. These are close signals. In lost deals, the prospect says "let me share this internally" and goes quiet.

---

**Stage 5: Contract + Implementation Alignment (Calls 5–7, Days 30–60)**

In won deals, this stage is almost administrative. The decision has already been made emotionally and financially by Call 4. What remains is SOW review, implementation scheduling, and sometimes a final "user adoption" demo for AP staff and department heads.

At Rio Verde, the deal closed 5 days after the pricing call — no additional calls needed. At Carmel, Stephanie asked Tyler to demo for department heads and staff — *"I may need you to demo it to a couple of the department heads and my staff so they can feel like they can ask questions"* — and Tyler offered to do it in person. At Boca Woods, the final team demo had 6 attendees (CFO, AP staff, Assistant Controller, Purchaser, POA manager) validating usability before the CFO signed off. At Denver, the process took longer (163 days total) because a GM needed to weigh in, but the outcome was never in doubt once Courtney had the pricing.

**The close is a non-event in won deals.** If it feels like a high-drama moment, the deal wasn't properly built.

### **Where Lost Deals Diverge (The Three Failure Gates)**

**Gate 1: Economic Buyer Absent (Call 1–2)** The person you're selling to can't actually buy. They need their GM, their board, their ownership group, or their parent company to approve. If the economic buyer never appears on a call, the deal is dead — it just doesn't know it yet. Elite Casino Resorts (Nicole Wright was Finance Director, not CFO — the real decision-maker "opposes cloud" and was never engaged), Beacon Hill Country Club (Mike Meissner was the only contact through 5 calls — no finance leader ever appeared), Magnolia Hospitality (single 22-minute call with President, then a 2-minute voicemail, then silence), and an estimated 63 of 108 lost deals all failed here. **This is a qualification problem — you're investing time in deals where the buyer isn't in the room.**

**Gate 2: Technical Knockout (Calls 2–3)** The prospect raises a must-have requirement that can't be answered live. If it's not resolved within 72 hours, the deal enters a death spiral. Elite Casino (PO matching with SWS), SeoulSpice (NetSuite native AP deemed sufficient at low volume), HCI Hospitality (bakery vendor EDI feeds — 3–5 weekly invoices × 40 locations that Ottimate couldn't automate), and Indianapolis Zoo (POS integration requirement) all died here. **This is the most controllable failure mode — it's a sales execution problem, not a market problem.**

**Gate 3: External Timing Block (Any Stage)** ERP migrations, incumbent contract lock-in, organizational turnover, and budget freezes. Fairfield Glade had *"Beanworks paid through the end of the year."* MasMex's CFO said *"It's going to be probably August before we really dig in."* Metropolitan Club SF's Controller said *"We're just turning around a period of mismanagement... I don't see this happening right this moment."* Multiple clubs cited fiscal year timing and finance committee cycles. **This is the least controllable — but it is predictable. Ask about these blockers on Call 1.**

### **The Messaging Misalignment**

Based on the transcripts, here's where your current messaging likely misses:

**What you're probably leading with:** Feature-led positioning. "Ottimate automates AP with AI-powered invoice capture, approval workflows, and vendor payments."

**What the prospect is thinking on Call 1:** "Do you know what it's like to be the only person entering every invoice by hand into Jonas?" or "Do you understand that my department heads won't touch a computer during busy season?" or "Has anyone else at a club like mine actually made this work?"

**What the prospect needs to hear on Call 1:** Proof that you've solved their exact problem for someone exactly like them. Rio Verde closed in 5 days because TrueClub was a trusted channel and the rebate math was immediate. Carmel closed because Stephanie had a peer reference and Tyler understood the Beanworks-to-Ottimate migration path. Denver closed because Katie could cite Admiral's Cove's month-end close improvement — a club Courtney could relate to.

**What the prospect is thinking on Call 4 (pricing):** "Can I justify this to my board/GM with a 30-second explanation?" They need a one-sentence ROI narrative: "This saves us one FTE and pays for itself in rebates" or "This cuts our month-end close by a week and eliminates our check fraud exposure."

**What they're not thinking about on any call:** Your AI capabilities, your OCR accuracy rates, or your platform architecture. These are table stakes that get validated during the demo. They never appear as decision drivers in any transcript.

## **Part 2: The ERP Landscape in Hospitality**

### **The Dominant Systems**

**Jonas Club Software** is the most prevalent ERP across your hospitality pipeline by a significant margin. It appeared in 46 of 204 deals (33 closed-won, 13 closed-lost) — making the Jonas ecosystem the dominant accounting platform in the country club and private club segment. Jonas handles club management, POS, member billing, and general ledger accounting in a single platform. Carmel Country Club, Denver Country Club, Boca Woods, Admiral's Cove, Raintree Country Club, and dozens of other club deals all run Jonas. The good news: Ottimate's Jonas integration is mature and was a positive factor in multiple closed-won deals. Jonas clubs convert at 72% — the second-highest win rate of any ERP in the dataset. The risk: Jonas has its own native invoice entry capability, and some clubs view adding Ottimate as adding complexity to a system they already know.

**NorthStar Club Management** appeared in 18 deals (14 closed-won, 4 closed-lost) — nearly all in country clubs and golf clubs. NorthStar is a direct Jonas competitor in the club management space, and Ottimate's integration with it was well-received. The 78% win rate is the highest of any ERP in the dataset. Fairfield Glade (lost) was a NorthStar shop but had Beanworks under contract — the loss was to the incumbent, not to NorthStar's native capabilities.

**ClubEssential** appeared in 18 deals (10 closed-won, 8 closed-lost) — the third major club management ERP. ClubEssential is particularly strong in larger metropolitan area clubs (Edina, Quail Hollow, Biltmore, Olympia Fields, Springs Club, DC Ranch). The 56% win rate is lower than Jonas and NorthStar because ClubEssential has somewhat more capable native AP workflows, which raises the bar for the prospect to feel enough pain to add Ottimate. When the pain is articulated (manual department-head coding, paper routing, approval bottlenecks), these deals close well.

**QuickBooks** appeared in 27 deals (13 closed-won, 14 closed-lost), mostly in smaller organizations — single-entity restaurants, smaller clubs, early-stage hospitality companies. Equator Coffees ran QuickBooks Online and closed quickly once they saw that QBO's approval add-on couldn't handle 400–500 invoices per week across 12 locations. QuickBooks organizations tend to be simpler deals with shorter cycles and lower ACV, but the pain is real — no approval workflows, no audit trail, no automated GL coding. Win rate is 48%.

**Sage Intacct** appeared in 14 deals (8 closed-won, 6 closed-lost). Unlike healthcare where Sage Intacct dominates, hospitality runs on specialized club management and hotel accounting systems. When Sage Intacct does appear, it's typically in multi-entity hospitality management companies and hotel groups — Sree Hotels, Discovery Land, Stepstone, Wedgewood Weddings. The 57% win rate reflects the strength of Ottimate's Intacct integration (proven in healthcare) when the prospect runs Intacct as their GL system of record.

**M3 / Infor** appeared in 14 deals (7 closed-won, 7 closed-lost). M3 is a hotel-specific accounting platform used by hotel management companies, and Infor is the broader parent company whose products appear across hospitality. The 50% win rate is better than previously estimated — Radiate Hospitality, Vivamee Hospitality, Stepstone, Coco Key Resort, and Main & Vine all closed successfully on M3/Infor. Losses were driven by multi-stakeholder complexity at hotel management companies, not integration concerns.

**Restaurant 365** appeared in 7 deals (3 closed-won, 4 closed-lost). R365 is increasingly the default restaurant operations platform for multi-unit chains. When Ottimate wins, it's because R365's native AP capabilities don't cover approval routing and vendor payment automation adequately. The 43% win rate shows these are competitive but winnable.

**NetSuite** appeared in 7 deals (3 closed-won, 4 closed-lost). The 43% win rate is nuanced — NetSuite deals are winnable when the prospect has 3+ entities and 200+ invoices/month, where NetSuite's native AP module falls short. But at lower complexity/volume, the bundled AP is "good enough."

**Great Plains** appeared in 5 deals (1 closed-won, 4 closed-lost). The 20% win rate reflects a legacy ERP where prospects are often planning migration, which stalls AP automation decisions.

**Sage 100/300 (Legacy)** appeared in 3 deals, all closed-lost. These are aging on-premise Sage products where the prospect may need ERP modernization before AP automation makes sense.

The real competitive threat comes from ERP-native AP modules (NetSuite, NorthStar's own capabilities) and incumbent contract lock-in (Beanworks at Fairfield Glade, Eden Red at Country Club of Darien).

### **What This Means for Pipeline Conversion**

**Your highest-conversion ERP targets are Jonas and NorthStar.** These club management systems have limited native AP automation, so the value proposition is unambiguous. If a prospect is on Jonas or NorthStar with multiple entities or departments, they are almost certainly in pain and Ottimate is a natural fit. Together these ERPs represent 64 deals and convert at 72–78% when they reach pricing.

**ClubEssential is your third club ERP — winnable but harder.** With 18 deals and a 56% win rate, ClubEssential prospects are reachable but the bar is higher because ClubEssential has somewhat more capable native AP workflows. The key is identifying prospects where department-head coding, paper routing, and approval bottlenecks exceed what the native system can handle — at that point, the pain drives conversion.

**QuickBooks is your velocity play but needs volume qualification.** It's the second most common system in your pipeline (27 deals), and the pain is clear (no workflows, no coding, no audit trail). But the 48% win rate reflects that QuickBooks shops are often smaller operations where the ACV may not justify the sales cycle. Qualify on invoice volume and multi-location complexity — single-entity QBO shops with under 100 invoices/month may not be worth pursuing.

**Sage Intacct is a mid-tier opportunity.** At 14 deals and a 57% win rate, Sage Intacct prospects convert well when the proven Intacct integration is positioned. These tend to be multi-entity hospitality management companies where the Intacct integration (already proven in healthcare) is a strong differentiator.

**Great Plains and legacy Sage are danger zones.** Great Plains converts at just 20% (1 of 5) and Sage 100/300 at 0% (0 of 3). These legacy ERPs often signal a prospect planning migration, which stalls all adjacent technology decisions. M3/Infor and NetSuite are more nuanced at 50% and 43% respectively — they're competitive but require qualifying on complexity (3+ entities, 200+ invoices/month) to filter out deals where native AP is "good enough."

## **Part 3: Connecting the Dots — Why Pipeline Isn't Converting**

The buyer journey and ERP data together tell a clear story about your pipeline-to-MRR conversion problem:

**1. You're spending cycles on deals that can't close.** An estimated 24 lost deals were mid-ERP-migration and were never going to buy during the evaluation window. Another 7+ were on ERPs where Ottimate has a near-0% win rate (Great Plains at 20%, Sage 100/300 at 0%, Acumatica at 0%). That's 31+ of 108 lost deals (~29%) that were fundamentally unqualifiable. If you eliminate these from pipeline, your effective win rate improves materially.

**2. The economic buyer isn't hearing the pitch.** In an estimated 63 of 108 lost deals, the CFO, GM, or board never engaged. Your AEs are building relationships with Controllers and AP managers who love the product but can't sign the check. The fix isn't better messaging — it's a hard gate: "No pricing call without CFO/GM confirmed." In won deals, the economic buyer was on the call by Stage 2 in 80% of cases. In lost deals, they were absent 58% of the time.

**3. The ROI case isn't being made.** Multiple lost deals had no ROI model delivered. The won deals that closed fastest all featured the prospect doing the math themselves — because the AE gave them the inputs. Rio Verde's Anthony said *"we're going to make money by doing this."* Avalon Yacht Club's Peggy compared to her current $12K spend. Build the ROI model before the pricing call: current check processing cost, current FTE time allocation, Ottimate cost, cashback offset, net savings.

**4. Warm channels convert; cold outreach doesn't.** The vast majority of won deals originated from TrueClub referrals, CMAA conference connections, or peer recommendations. Cold outbound deals stall at higher rates because the trust hasn't been established. Investing more in the TrueClub partnership, CMAA and NCA conference presence, and customer-to-prospect referral programs would tilt pipeline toward higher-converting deal origins.

**5. Your best segment (country clubs on Jonas/NorthStar/ClubEssential) is your competitive moat.** Jonas and NorthStar deals convert at 72–78%, and ClubEssential at 56%. Together these three club ERPs account for 82 deals — 40% of total pipeline. Country clubs have the simplest buying process (Controller + GM, not a 10-person committee), the strongest referral network, and the most acute pain (thin staffing, paper-heavy, seasonal approval bottlenecks). Tilting pipeline generation toward country clubs and private clubs on club management ERPs would materially improve conversion rates.
