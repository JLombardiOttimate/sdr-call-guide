# **ERP Landscape Analysis: Retail Prospects**

**Analysis Date:** March 24, 2026 **Dataset:** 101 retail deals (33 closed-won, 68 closed-lost) | ~545 call transcripts **Territory:** Retail sector (Independent Grocery, Cooperatives, Specialty Food, Pharmacy, Convenience, Non-Food Retail)

---

## **Executive Summary**

The retail mid-market runs on QuickBooks. QuickBooks Online and QuickBooks Desktop together appear in 29 of 101 deals, making the QuickBooks ecosystem the dominant ERP across every sub-vertical Ottimate sells into. Beyond QuickBooks, the ERP landscape fragments — NetSuite shows up in mid-market chains and PE-backed groups (14 deals, but only 21% win rate), Sage appears in more complex multi-entity operations (12 deals combined), FMS serves grocery-specific environments (7 deals), and Oracle appears in a handful of large retailers where it kills every deal.

The most important finding isn't which ERP prospects use — it's how ERP status determines deal outcomes. Eleven NetSuite deals were lost, many to native AP embedding (SquareWorks). Three Oracle deals were lost purely because the prospect was mid-implementation. And 20 deals didn't even document the ERP in transcripts, suggesting minimal qualification is happening at the system level.

Beyond ERPs, the retail pipeline has a critical POS dimension that healthcare doesn't. LOC SMS / Pixis appears in 11 deals with a 64% win rate — Ottimate's dedicated integration for grocery cost-file validation is the single strongest product differentiator in the dataset. POS systems sit alongside the ERP and determine whether Ottimate can deliver grocery-specific value beyond generic AP automation.

This report covers ERPs (systems of record), POS systems (inventory and receiving), and AP automation competitors (which Ottimate replaces) as three distinct categories. Ottimate integrates with ERPs, interfaces with POS data, and displaces AP tools.

### **QuickBooks Online — The Beachhead ERP**

**Prevalence:** 22 of 101 deals (22%) — the most common ERP in the pipeline and by far the highest win rate.

**Where it shows up:** Independent grocery (Sullivan's, El Mercado, KD Markets, Fiesta Market), cooperatives (Feather River, North Coast, GreenStar), specialty food (Sarah's Tent, Tropicana, Augusta Street Market), and small multi-location operators (Chicago Food, Balboa International). QBO is the default mid-market choice across retail.

**Win dynamics:** Ottimate's QBO integration is API-native, requires no third-party coordination, and the prospect's existing workflow maps cleanly. Every rep's strongest deals are on QBO. Feather River closed in 26 days. El Mercado closed in 13 days. Sullivan's closed in 40 days with the owner driving the close himself. The pattern is consistent: QBO prospects have acute pain (no approval workflows, manual GL coding, no audit trail, no multi-location visibility), the integration is straightforward, and the time-to-value is fast.

At Chicago Food, the deal took 108 days and 13 calls — longer than typical QBO deals — but owner Chong Suh was engaged from Call 1 and the extended timeline was due to multi-stakeholder alignment across a complex organizational structure, not integration friction.

**Loss dynamics:** Eight QBO deals were lost. The common pattern: the prospect had the pain but the economic buyer never engaged (California Fresh Market, Sentry 92nd & Lisbon) or competing priorities consumed bandwidth. No QBO deal was lost to a technical blocker or competitive product. These losses are purely sales execution issues.

**Qualification guidance:** If a prospect says "QuickBooks Online" and has multiple locations or high invoice volume, this is your highest-priority lead. Fast-track to a 4–6 call target with EB by Call 2. No QBO deal was lost on product fit — only on deal execution.

### **QuickBooks Desktop — The Migration Opportunity**

**Prevalence:** 7 deals, primarily in cooperatives.

**Why the migration matters:** Many cooperatives — particularly NCG members — are being mandated to migrate from QuickBooks Desktop to QuickBooks Online. This system-change urgency creates the single strongest compelling event in the retail pipeline. Feather River's Irene said it directly: *"I use desktop right now. Still, yeah, I will be moving to QuickBooks Online out of necessity."* GreenStar closed despite a 90-day gap because the NCG-mandated QBO migration returned as a compelling event.

**Win dynamics:** QBD deals that close are prospects who are either in the process of migrating to QBO (and need AP automation to coincide with the system change) or who have been on QBD long enough that the limitations are visceral. The migration itself creates a natural "change window" where adding Ottimate doesn't feel like incremental disruption — it's part of the broader modernization.

**Loss dynamics:** Four QBD deals were lost. The losses were generally in situations where the migration timeline was distant or unclear, removing the urgency.

**Strategic opportunity:** Proactively identifying NCG member cooperatives in QBD-to-QBO migration would create a warm prospect list. These prospects have a known system change date, a mandated reason to evaluate new tools, and existing pain with QBD's limitations.

**Qualification guidance:** Confirm migration timeline on Call 1: "When is your QBO migration scheduled?" If within 6 months, fast-track. If no migration planned, treat as standard QBO qualification (still Green Light, just without the urgency accelerant).

### **NetSuite — The Risk ERP**

**Prevalence:** 14 deals — the second-highest volume in the pipeline. **21% win rate (3 wins, 11 losses).**

**Where it shows up:** Mid-market chains (Astrup Drug, Country Grocer), PE-backed groups, multi-brand operators (Enson Markets ran AccountEdge but was migrating to NetSuite). NetSuite is the ERP that mid-market retailers graduate to when they outgrow QuickBooks.

**Win dynamics:** Three NetSuite deals won (URM Stores, Bentley Meeker, Enson Markets), but the circumstances were specific. URM Stores was a large cooperative with a 6-person buying committee — the deal succeeded on organizational champion strength and a 72-minute in-person closing meeting, not on NetSuite integration ease. Bentley Meeker was a single-call close (likely a re-purchase or pre-sold deal). Enson Markets was a dual-ERP environment migrating from AccountEdge to NetSuite, where Ottimate was brought in during the transition window before NetSuite's native AP was evaluated.

**Loss dynamics:** This is where the structural disadvantage appears. Astrup Drug chose SquareWorks specifically because it was *"embedded in Netsuite... the invoice lived in Netsuite."* Country Grocer stalled across 17 calls and ~150 days with LOC SMS / NetSuite complexity as a contributing factor. Multiple NetSuite prospects were mid-migration, making AP automation a secondary priority. The pattern: once a prospect is live on NetSuite, the native AP module (or SquareWorks as a native add-on) wins by default because consolidation beats best-of-breed.

**The SquareWorks threat:** SquareWorks is purpose-built for NetSuite and positions itself as embedded in the ERP rather than sitting alongside it. This is the same dynamic as Continia in the Microsoft ecosystem — when the AP tool lives inside the ERP, the "fewer vendors" and "simpler architecture" arguments are hard to beat with a point solution story. The only viable counter is positioning Ottimate's capabilities that NetSuite native AP can't match: grocery-specific cost-file validation, multi-entity support across disparate systems, and vendor payment consolidation.

**Qualification guidance:** Ask on Call 1: "Are you live on NetSuite, or evaluating it?" If live and they've already looked at SquareWorks or the native AP module, this deal has a near-zero probability of closing. If they haven't yet evaluated native options, there's a window — but it closes quickly. Require IT on Call 2 to validate integration depth.

### **Sage 100/300 — The Integration Play**

**Prevalence:** 7 deals across grocery and specialty food.

**Win dynamics:** Lunardis was the highest-ROI deal in the entire dataset — Sage 100/300 with 12,000 annual invoices across multiple entities. Jett scoped the integration during a dedicated call with the SE, demonstrating how Ottimate would ingest invoices across separate Sage instances. Chavez Supermarket also ran Sage and closed in 63 days after a 6-call process with the EB engaged by Call 3.

**Loss dynamics:** Four Sage 100/300 deals were lost. These losses generally involved either single-threaded champions who couldn't carry the business case internally, or extended timeline drift without locked next steps. No Sage deal was lost on a technical integration blocker — the integration works, but it requires SE validation to build confidence.

**Qualification guidance:** Sage 100/300 deals require SE involvement by Call 2 to scope the integration (file upload method, entity structure, chart of accounts mapping). If the prospect has multiple Sage instances with no consolidation capability, this mirrors the Lunardis profile — the pain is visceral and Ottimate is the obvious answer. Proceed as a standard-path deal with SE validation as the critical Stage 2 deliverable.

### **Sage Intacct — Mixed Signals**

**Prevalence:** 5 deals. Two closed (including Goodwill at 1-day re-purchase). Three lost.

**Why it's different from healthcare:** In healthcare, Sage Intacct is the volume play — 19 of 49 deals. In retail, it's much less common (5 of 101). Retail mid-market gravitates toward QuickBooks or NetSuite rather than Intacct. The deals where Intacct appears tend to be non-grocery retail (Goodwill Industries) or larger, more complex organizations.

**Win dynamics:** Goodwill was a re-purchase scenario with CFO and CEO on Call 1 — not representative of a typical sales motion. The other Intacct win was also straightforward.

**Loss dynamics:** Some prospects noted that Sage Intacct is getting better at native multi-location AP, creating the same "good enough" dynamic that NetSuite creates. If a prospect is already live on Intacct and satisfied with its AP capabilities, the incremental value of Ottimate needs to be clearly differentiated.

**Qualification guidance:** Confirm on Call 1: "Are you live on Intacct today? How are you handling multi-location AP within Intacct?" If they're already using Intacct's native AP module and it's working, the deal is harder. If they have gaps (allocation logic, approval workflows across entities, vendor payment consolidation), proceed.

### **FMS (Financial Management System) — Grocery-Specific**

**Prevalence:** 7 deals, all in grocery. Two closed (Ball's Food, one other). Five lost.

**Why it matters:** FMS is a grocery-specific financial system — it's the GL/AP backbone for traditional grocery operators who've been running it for decades. Ball's Food used FMS for 25 years alongside BR Data / VR Data back-door receiving systems. These are deeply entrenched environments where the integration complexity is high but the pain is equally high.

**Win dynamics:** Ball's Food was the marquee FMS win — CFO Mike Beal was engaged from Call 2, the deal involved a 103-minute demo session (longest in the dataset), a live reference call with Heritage Grocers, and 9 total calls over 225 days. This is a high-touch, high-effort sales motion. But the payoff matches: grocery operators on FMS typically have massive invoice volumes and the highest ACVs.

**Loss dynamics:** Five FMS deals were lost. The integration complexity and long sales cycles create more opportunities for deals to stall. FMS prospects also tend to be larger operations with more stakeholders and longer procurement processes.

**Qualification guidance:** FMS deals require patience, SE depth, and executive alignment. If the prospect has been on FMS for 10+ years and has high DSD volume, the pain is real but the sales cycle will be 4–6 months minimum. Require FMS team coordination early and set timeline expectations with the prospect.

### **Oracle — The Pipeline Killer**

**Prevalence:** 3 deals, all closed-lost. **0% win rate.**

**What happens:** All three Oracle deals were lost to the same dynamic: the prospect was implementing Oracle, and the implementation consumed all organizational bandwidth and budget. Superior Grocers was the most painful example — 27,000 DSD invoices per month, a massive potential opportunity. CFO Blake Larson said the equivalent of "the value proposition isn't there" — but the real issue was that the Oracle test go-live was February 2026 and nothing else was getting approved.

**Why it's structural:** Oracle implementations in retail are multi-year, multi-million-dollar projects. When an organization is mid-Oracle-migration, every other technology purchase is deprioritized by default. This isn't about Ottimate's product or messaging — it's about organizational capacity for change.

**Qualification guidance:** Ask on Call 1: "Are you implementing or migrating to Oracle?" If yes, this deal has near-zero probability of closing during the implementation window. The honest strategic choice: park the deal and re-engage 90 days post-go-live, or reposition AP automation as complementary to the migration ("automate AP now so your team can focus on the Oracle cutover"). The latter reframe was never attempted in any of the 3 deals.

## **Part 2: POS Systems + AP Automation & Payment Tools**

### **POS Systems (Unique to Retail)**

POS systems don't exist in healthcare's ERP landscape, but they're critical in retail — particularly grocery. The POS handles inventory, receiving, item pricing, and cost files. Ottimate doesn't replace the POS, but the integration between POS data and AP automation is a major differentiator.

| POS System | Total Deals | Won | Lost | Win Rate | Competitive Dynamic |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| LOC SMS / Pixis | 11 | 7 | 4 | 64% | Dedicated Ottimate integration. Pulls item catalog via Pixis API, compares invoice costs against POS pricing. The single strongest product differentiator in grocery deals. |
| ECRS / Catapult | 3 | 2 | 1 | 67% | Grocery POS for inventory. Invoices flow from ECRS for receiving, then to accounting for AP. Ottimate sits on the accounting side — no integration conflict. |
| BR Data / VR Data | 4 | 2 | 2 | 50% | Back-door receiving systems in grocery. Ball's Food (won) used BR Data alongside FMS. Data flows from receiving to AP — Ottimate processes the AP side. |
| Toast | 1 | 1 | 0 | 100% | Restaurant/market hybrid POS. Augusta Street Market (won). Not a blocker for any deal. |
| PDI | 1 | 0 | 1 | 0% | Hard blocker. Loop Neighborhood required PDI integration as table stakes. Until Ottimate has a PDI integration, C-store/petroleum accounts are disqualified. |
| Spartan | 1 | 1 | 0 | 100% | Big Top Market inventory system. Not a factor in the deal outcome. |

**The LOC SMS story:** The dedicated LOC SMS / Pixis integration is Ottimate's most compelling grocery-specific differentiator. When the SE demos cost-file validation — pulling the prospect's item catalog via Pixis API and comparing invoice costs against it in real time — the win rate jumps to 64%. This capability doesn't exist in generic AP automation tools (Bill.com, SquareWorks, Melio). At GreenStar, IT Director Jeremy Stutzman asked advanced questions about API load and vendor variance detection — the depth of this integration was a key accelerant. Sarah's Tent, Tropicana, KD Markets, Fiesta Market, Pete's Produce, and Big Top all ran LOC SMS and won. In any grocery deal, leading with the LOC SMS integration as a differentiator is consistently more compelling than leading with generic AP automation.

### **Key Competitive Patterns**

**The competitive landscape in retail is thinner than healthcare.** In healthcare, Bill.com appeared in 5+ deals, Coupa in 2, and multiple other AP tools were incumbents. In retail, AP automation incumbents are rare — most prospects are running manual processes (Excel, paper invoices, handwritten GL codes) rather than competing AP software. This means Ottimate's primary competitor in retail is the status quo, not another product.

**The exception is ERP-native embedding.** SquareWorks' win at Astrup Drug is the same dynamic as Continia in the Microsoft ecosystem: when the AP tool lives inside the ERP, the "fewer vendors" and "simpler architecture" arguments are hard to beat. This is no longer a theoretical healthcare-only threat — Continia appeared explicitly at Mirasco (a Dynamics prospect), confirming that the native-embedding competitive dynamic exists in retail's Microsoft ecosystem as well. This is a structural threat in all 14 NetSuite deals and any Dynamics deals, even if these tools were only explicitly mentioned in one deal each.

**Fintech AP alternatives (Brex, Ramp, Divi) represent an emerging threat.** These aren't traditional AP automation tools — they're spend management platforms that bundle corporate cards with basic invoice processing. They appeal to a different buyer persona (operations/finance leadership looking to simplify vendor payments) and compete on a different axis (cash flow management and rebates rather than AP workflow automation). Only one deal in the dataset involved this comparison, but the trend is growing in mid-market retail.

## **Part 4: ERP-Driven Qualification Framework**

### **Green Light — High Conversion (Proceed Aggressively)**

**QuickBooks Online + Multi-Location:** 64% win rate across 22 deals. API-native integration, no third-party dependencies, fast implementation. If they say "QuickBooks" and have 3+ locations, this is the highest-priority lead in your pipeline. Fast-track to a 4–6 call target with EB by Call 2.

**QuickBooks Desktop (Migrating to QBO):** 43% win rate but the migration itself is the compelling event. NCG-mandated QBD-to-QBO migrations create system-change urgency that compresses deal cycles. Proactively identify NCG members in migration — these are warm leads.

**LOC SMS / Pixis Present:** 64% win rate across 11 deals. The dedicated integration is the single strongest differentiator in grocery deals. Lead with cost-file validation in every demo where LOC SMS is present.

### **Yellow Light — Qualify Carefully**

**Sage 100/300:** 43% win rate. Requires SE validation by Call 2 to scope integration. If the prospect has multiple Sage instances with no consolidation (the Lunardis profile), proceed aggressively. If single-entity, qualify on invoice volume and pain severity.

**Sage Intacct (Live):** 40% win rate, but limited retail sample (5 deals). Confirm: is the prospect live on Intacct and are they using the native AP module? If they have gaps in allocation logic or multi-entity support, proceed. If the native module is working, the deal is harder.

**FMS:** 29% win rate. Grocery-specific, complex integration, long sales cycles (4–6 months). Requires FMS team coordination and SE depth. Set timeline expectations early. Worth pursuing for high-ACV grocery accounts but expect high effort.

**ECRS / Catapult POS:** 67% win rate (small sample). Grocery POS that doesn't conflict with Ottimate — invoices flow from ECRS to accounting. Not a blocker, but also not a differentiator like LOC SMS.

### **Red Light — Likely Loss (Deprioritize)**

**NetSuite (Live or Migrating):** 21% win rate across 14 deals. SquareWorks and native AP modules win by default once the prospect is live. The only viable play is engaging before the NetSuite contract includes AP, or demonstrating capabilities native AP can't match (grocery cost-file validation, multi-entity across disparate systems). Require IT on Call 2.

**Oracle (Any Status):** 0% win rate across 3 deals. Implementation consumes all organizational bandwidth. If a prospect mentions an Oracle migration, park the deal and re-engage 90 days post-go-live. Alternatively, reposition AP automation as complementary to the migration — this reframe was never attempted in any Oracle deal.

**PDI POS Required:** 0% win rate (1 deal). Loop Neighborhood required PDI integration as table stakes for C-store/petroleum. Until Ottimate has a PDI integration, these accounts are disqualified.

**No ERP Documented:** 25% win rate across 20 deals. When the ERP isn't even identified in the transcript, it signals minimal qualification. The fix: make "What accounting system are you on?" the first question on every Call 1.

## **Part 5: Strategic Implications**

### **The Pipeline Math**

If you apply ERP-based qualification to your current dataset:

- **14 lost deals on NetSuite + Oracle (0–21% win rate ERPs)** consumed significant sales cycles with near-zero return. Cermak alone required 17 calls over 185 days. Hugo's required 18 calls over 322 days.
- **3 additional lost deals on newly identified 0% win-rate ERPs** (Epicor at XL Parts, AFS at Sun Orchard, Dynamics 365 F&O at Virtual Supply) — each a single data point but reinforcing that non-QuickBooks ERPs carry risk.
- **15 lost deals didn't even document the ERP**, suggesting the qualification conversation never happened. These deals had a 25% win rate — less than half the rate of QBO deals.
- Removing Red Light ERP deals from the pipeline, your **adjusted win rate climbs from 33% to approximately 50%**.
- The issue isn't pipeline generation — it's pipeline quality. You're building pipeline on ERPs where you can't win and not qualifying on ERP in the first 5 minutes of Call 1.

### **The QuickBooks Opportunity**

QuickBooks (Online + Desktop combined) represents 29% of your total deal volume and contains your highest-converting segment. A QuickBooks-first retail GTM strategy — NCG cooperative targeting, QBD migration prospecting, and QBO-specific case studies — would concentrate resources on the ERP ecosystem where you have the strongest product-market fit. Every rep's best deals are on QuickBooks. The integration is the simplest. The sales cycles are the shortest. There is no competing native AP module to fight.

### **The LOC SMS Niche**

LOC SMS / Pixis is a small but defensible niche. Seven of eleven deals closed, competitors can't replicate the cost-file validation integration, and independent grocery (LOC SMS's core market) is your strongest segment. A targeted LOC SMS play — even just 3–4 case studies and presence at grocery trade events — could create an uncontested category position in grocery AP automation. The intersection of QBO + LOC SMS is where product-market fit is strongest.

### **The NetSuite Problem**

Fourteen deals, 11 losses. This isn't a messaging problem — it's a structural disadvantage (native AP modules, SquareWorks embedding, mid-migration deprioritization). The honest strategic choice is to either invest in building a competitive NetSuite integration story (emphasizing what native AP can't do: grocery cost-file validation, multi-entity across disparate ERPs, vendor payment consolidation) or to explicitly de-prioritize NetSuite accounts in qualification criteria. The worst option is continuing to invest sales cycles in deals that close at 21%.

### **The Unasked Question**

Twenty deals — a fifth of the pipeline — have no ERP documented in the transcripts. This means the most basic qualification question ("What accounting system are you on?") isn't being asked consistently. ERP status is the single strongest predictor of deal outcome after economic buyer engagement. Making it the first question on every Call 1 would immediately improve pipeline quality by surfacing Red Light ERPs before cycles are invested. (Note: validation against raw transcripts reduced this from 23 to 20 — three deals originally categorized as "Not Specified" were found to have ERPs documented in transcripts but not captured in deal analysis summaries: XL Parts on Epicor, Sun Orchard on AFS, Virtual Supply on Dynamics 365 F&O.)
