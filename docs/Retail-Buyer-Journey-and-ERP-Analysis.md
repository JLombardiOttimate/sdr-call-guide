# **Retail Buyer Journey & ERP Landscape Analysis**

**Analysis Date:** March 24, 2026 **Dataset:** 101 retail deals (33 closed-won, 68 closed-lost) | 545 call transcripts

---

## **Part 1: The Actual Buyer Journey (What Really Happens, Call by Call)**

Your hypothesis about messaging misalignment is supported by the data, but the problem is more specific than "wrong messaging." The issue is that **your sales motion assumes a linear, seller-controlled journey, but retail buyers follow a fragmented, operationally-driven journey where the economic buyer is structurally absent until it's too late.** The prospect's AP person or operations director feels the pain viscerally, but the owner or CFO — who signs the check — never hears the pitch in 63% of lost deals. Here's what the transcripts actually show.

### **The Winning Journey (Observed in 28 of 33 Closed-Won Deals)**

**Stage 1: Warm Entry + Pain Validation (Call 1, Days 1–7)**

The first call in won deals is overwhelmingly owner-present. Twelve of thirty-three closed-won deals had the economic buyer on Call 1 itself — and those deals closed at a dramatically higher rate than the rest of the pipeline. Goodwill came through a re-purchase (CFO Chris Howard and CEO Vicky Burton on the call). Feather River came through an NCG cooperative connection with Irene (Finance Manager) who had budget authority. Big Top Market's owner Avin was the initial contact and immediately began negotiating trial terms.

What happens on this call: The AE meets 1–3 people (usually the Finance Manager, Operations Director, or Owner). The conversation is exploratory — the prospect describes their current process, the AE asks about volume, systems, and pain. The critical signal on this call is **who's in the room and what questions they ask.** In won deals, the owner or CFO attended Call 1 in 36% of cases — but they asked questions about cost, ROI, and timeline, not just "how does it work."

The prospect's mindset at this stage: "I know I have a problem, and I'm checking whether you understand my world." They're evaluating whether Ottimate speaks their language (grocery DSD, multi-store GL coding, LOC SMS cost files, cooperative invoice volume) — not evaluating features yet.

*"I'm like all over the place just with payments... sometimes five different ways to pay people"* — Irene, Finance Manager, Feather River Food Coop (Won)

*"That's pretty much a full time job"* — Rosa, Tropicana Supermarkets (Won)

*"We actually had our power disconnected at a location due to 14-day payment delays"* — Paige Drummond, COO, Goodwill Industries (Won — same-day close)

**What triggers the next call:** The AE proposes a demo with the broader team. In won deals, the AE specifically asks who else should attend and gets names. The call is scheduled before the current call ends. In lost deals, Call 1 ends with "we'll circle back" or "let me talk to my team." Of the 68 lost deals, 19 never progressed past a single call — Boot World, Clearlight Saunas, A.I. Foods, Stewart's Food Stores, and others — every one ended without a locked next step.

---

**Stage 2: Team Demo + "Aha Moment" (Calls 2–3, Days 7–21)**

This is the most important call in the entire journey. In won deals, the demo call expands from 1–2 people to 3–6. The AE brings a Solutions Engineer. The prospect brings their controller, IT director, sometimes the owner.

What happens: The SE walks through Ottimate using scenarios that mirror the prospect's actual workflow. At GreenStar, Joseph Lombardi demonstrated the LOC SMS / Pixis integration — pulling the item catalog via API and comparing invoice costs against it in real time. At Ball's Food, a 103-minute demo session covered FMS integration, DSD receiving workflows, and virtual card rebate mechanics. At Lunardis, Jett scoped 12,000 annual invoices across Sage 100/300 entities. At Chicago Food, Tommy Jee (IT) provided deep technical input across 5 calls.

The "aha moment" is visible in the transcripts. At GreenStar, Eric asked *"Our team is scanning invoices in PDF... what's the actual outcome? Is that generating a price change batch file?"* — he was already testing implementation logistics, not evaluating whether the product works. At Ball's Food, Mike Beal (CFO) asked *"What would be the terms of success? Is this it right here?"* — testing commitment framework. At Sarah's Tent, Alexandra connected automation directly to inventory accuracy: *"If an invoice is coming in twice and being put in the system... making our inventory not accurate."*

**The prospect's mindset shifts here from "do you understand my world?" to "could this actually work for us?"**

In lost deals, this is where the journey diverges. Hugo's had 4 substantive calls with strong engagement — then proposed an "Aug 7 team meeting" with Finance that never confirmed, followed by 8-month silence. Cermak ran 5 calls with Martin (operations) but owner Mike never heard the pitch. Brooks Grocery's COO Zach was enthusiastic but neither he nor AP Manager Darby had budget authority. Berkot's ran 5 calls with 3 senior stakeholders and then lost with no documented reason.

**What triggers the next call:** In won deals, the prospect asks about pricing, implementation timeline, or integration specifics. These are buying signals — they're mentally moving from "could this work?" to "what would it take?" Two things consistently happen in won deals: a scoping exercise gets initiated (chart of accounts, invoice samples, vendor list), and a private champion conversation surfaces hidden concerns. At Lunardis (Call 3), Jett had a 1-on-1 sync with Alex where Alex revealed cost concerns and internal timeline pressure. At Big Top (Call 1), Avin asked about a 3-month trial directly.

---

**Stage 3: Pricing + ROI Justification (Calls 3–6, Days 14–40)**

This is the call where deals are won or lost — not because of price, but because of **who hears the price and what context surrounds it.**

In every fast-closing won deal, the owner or CFO was on the pricing call. At Goodwill, CEO Vicky Burton said *"Yeah. We'll do it... send it to Chris and he'll get my signature"* — same-day close. At Augusta Street Market, owner NP said *"I mean, that feels fair to me... I feel pretty comfortable with that"* — on pricing. At Sullivan's Grocery, owner Parker Sullivan drove the close himself: *"Next steps, say we make an agreement today."* At Lunardis, Alex reported *"Tony gave him the green light. He just needs today to finish some stuff and he's going to look at it tomorrow morning and sign it."*

The winning pattern on pricing calls: the AE presents a quantified business case before or alongside the price. At Ball's Food, CFO Mike Beal explicitly requested "hard numbers on ROI as well as what does it do to help our business." At Lunardis, a 301% ROI case was presented. At Renewal by Andersen, Daniel Bishop (Director of Finance) evaluated pricing across 14 calls but had financial context throughout.

In lost deals, pricing was presented without this context. At Superior Grocers, CFO Blake Larson said *"If I save $5M a year, then a spend of $200-250K would be a no-brainer. But we don't see that value proposition"* — no ROI model was ever delivered. At NuCara Pharmacy, the CFO said *"I wish we would have known that you were going to charge 80,000 dollars."* At Cermak, Martin reported the owner's reaction: *"Even if it's half price, he's still going to say we're not looking at it"* — price was never the real objection because the owner never heard the value case.

**The prospect's mindset: "Is this worth the money, the disruption, and my political capital to push through?"**

**What triggers the next call:** In won deals, the prospect asks about contract terms, implementation start dates, or who their CSM will be. These are close signals. In lost deals, the prospect says "let me share this internally" and goes quiet.

---

**Stage 4: Contract + Implementation Alignment (Calls 5–10, Days 30–60+)**

In won deals, this stage is almost administrative. The decision has already been made emotionally and financially by Stage 3. What remains is SOW review, implementation scheduling, and sometimes a final executive alignment call.

At Ball's Food, Calls 6–9 were implementation scoping after the CFO committed. At McCaffrey's, the final 5 calls (of 15 total) were reference calls, in-person meetings, and implementation planning — no new objections. At Lunardis, Tony (owner) signed within 15 days of his green light. At URM Stores, a 72-minute in-person final meeting sealed the deal.

**The close is a non-event in won deals.** If it feels like a high-drama moment, the deal wasn't properly built.

Close accelerators across all 33 won deals: in-person meetings (8 deals, correlated with largest ACVs), reference calls (Ball's Food leveraged Heritage Grocers as a live reference), Director-level seller engagement (Pat Burkley appeared on calls in Lunardis, Chavez, Fresh Farms, Sullivan's, Fiesta Market, GreenStar, and others), and private champion conversations that surfaced hidden concerns early.

### **Where Lost Deals Diverge (The Three Failure Gates)**

**Gate 1: Demo-to-Business-Case Gap (Calls 2–4)** This is the deadliest gate in the retail pipeline. Of 82 deals that reach Stage 2, only 45 progress to Stage 3 — a **45% attrition rate.** Fourteen deals stalled because no next step was locked after the demo (Hugo's, Food City, Mackenthun's, Geritom, Raja Food, Berkot's). Nine died because the champion couldn't sell internally (Cermak, Maria Pinto, Trig's, Sin Lee Food, Wessels Oil). Eight disappeared into extended silence of 30+ days (California Fresh Market at 9 months, Swanson's at 6 months, Hugo's at 8 months). **This is the most controllable failure mode — it's a sales execution problem, not a market problem.**

**Gate 2: Champion Power Gap (Calls 3–6)** The person you're selling to can't actually buy. They need their owner, their CFO, their board, or their cooperative management to approve. If the economic buyer never appears on a call, the deal is dead — it just doesn't know it yet. At Cermak, Martin (operations) ran 17 calls over 185 days but owner Mike never directly engaged — Martin reported *"He just basically shot it down and said, oh, this isn't the time right now."* At NuCara, Kristen (AP) eventually said *"I feel like I'm wasting your time having these calls when I don't have any information for you."* Across all 68 lost deals, **43 (63%) never had the economic buyer on any call.** In contrast, 85% of won deals had EB engagement by Call 3. **This is a qualification problem — you're investing time in deals where the buyer isn't in the room.**

**Gate 3: External Timing Block (Any Stage)** ERP migrations, competing priorities, organizational turnover, and cash flow constraints. Twenty-seven lost deals cited competing priorities. Oracle implementations killed 3 deals outright (0% win rate). NetSuite migrations created secondary-priority dynamics. Navjoot at 92nd & Lisbon Sentry said *"Timing wise not a good fit... still slow... still letting people go... not ready for extra services."* **This is the least controllable — but it is predictable. Ask about these blockers on Call 1.**

### **The Messaging Misalignment**

Based on the transcripts, here's where your current messaging likely misses:

**What you're probably leading with:** Feature-led positioning. "Ottimate automates AP with AI-powered invoice capture, approval workflows, and vendor payments."

**What the prospect is thinking on Call 1:** "Do you understand that I have 6 stores with no visibility into what's getting approved at each location?" or "Do you know what it's like to have one bookkeeper manually entering 800 invoices a month across QuickBooks?"

**What the prospect needs to hear on Call 1:** Proof that you've solved their exact problem for someone exactly like them. Feather River closed in 26 days because the NCG cooperative network provided trust. Ball's Food closed because Heritage Grocers appeared as a live reference. GreenStar closed because the LOC SMS integration demo proved Ottimate understood their specific POS-to-accounting workflow.

**What the prospect is thinking on Stage 3 (pricing):** "Can I justify this to my owner/board with a 30-second explanation?" They need a one-sentence ROI narrative: "This eliminates your AP clerk role and pays for itself in vendor rebates" or "This cuts your month-end close by 3 days and gives you real-time visibility across all locations."

**What they're not thinking about on any call:** Your AI capabilities, your OCR accuracy rates, or your platform architecture. These are table stakes that get validated during the demo. They never appear as decision drivers in any transcript.

## **Part 2: The ERP Landscape in Retail**

### **The Dominant Systems**

**QuickBooks Online** is the most prevalent ERP across the retail pipeline by a significant margin. It appeared in 22 of 101 deals with a 64% win rate — the highest of any ERP in the dataset. In independent grocery, cooperatives, specialty food, and small chains, QBO is the default choice. Feather River, North Coast Co-Op, Sullivan's, El Mercado, Chicago Food, Sarah's Tent, Tropicana, and others all run QBO. The good news: Ottimate's QBO integration is API-native, requires no third-party coordination, and the prospect's existing workflow maps cleanly. Every rep's strongest deals are on QBO. The risk is minimal — the only competitive threat is from fintech alternatives (Ramp, Brex) positioning as simpler corporate cards with basic AP.

**QuickBooks Desktop** appeared in 7 deals with a 43% win rate. The critical dynamic here is migration: many cooperatives are being mandated by NCG to move from QBD to QBO, creating system-change urgency that acts as a compelling event. Feather River's Irene said it directly — she'll be moving to QBO "out of necessity." Proactively identifying NCG member cooperatives in migration would create a warm prospect list.

**NetSuite** appeared in 14 deals — the second-highest volume — but with only a 21% win rate (3 wins, 11 losses). NetSuite is the risk ERP. The Astrup Drug loss is instructive — they chose SquareWorks specifically because it was "embedded in Netsuite." Native ERP-embedded AP automation is a competitive threat Ottimate hasn't addressed in NetSuite accounts. NetSuite prospects are also frequently mid-migration, making AP automation a secondary priority.

**Sage (100/300 and Intacct combined)** appeared in 12 deals. Sage 100/300 appeared in 7 deals at 43% win rate — Lunardis (the highest-ROI deal in the dataset) ran Sage 100/300 with 12,000 annual invoices. Sage Intacct appeared in 5 deals at 40% — won Goodwill (re-purchase) but some prospects noted Intacct was getting better at native multi-location AP.

**FMS (Financial Management System)** appeared in 7 deals at 29% win rate. FMS is grocery-specific — Ball's Food used FMS for 25 years alongside BR Data / VR Data back-door receiving systems. Integration is complex but proven when it works.

**Oracle** appeared in 3 deals. **0% win rate.** All 3 deals lost because the prospect was implementing Oracle and AP automation was deprioritized. Superior Grocers — 27,000 DSD invoices per month, a massive opportunity — was lost because the Oracle go-live consumed all organizational bandwidth.

**Other ERPs** appeared in single deals each: Great Plains/Dynamics GP (Woodlands Market, won), Epicor (XL Parts, lost), AFS/Advanced Food Systems (Sun Orchard, lost), and Dynamics 365 F&O (Virtual Supply, lost). These are isolated data points but reinforce that non-QuickBooks ERPs carry incremental risk.

**Key takeaway:** AP automation competitors are a secondary threat in retail — the real competitive risk is ERP-native solutions like SquareWorks (NetSuite) and Continia (Dynamics), which win on a "simpler, embedded" pitch. SquareWorks won Astrup Drug; Continia was evaluated alongside Ottimate at Mirasco, confirming the native-embedding threat exists in both NetSuite and Microsoft ecosystems in retail. In non-NetSuite/non-Dynamics accounts, Ottimate's grocery specialization advantage (LOC SMS integration, cost-file validation, multi-store GL mapping) is strong. In ERP-native accounts, the counter-positioning needs to emphasize what native AP can't do: multi-entity support across disparate systems, grocery-specific cost validation, and vendor payment consolidation.

### **What This Means for Pipeline Conversion**

**Your highest-conversion ERP target is QuickBooks Online.** At 64% win rate across 22 deals, QBO shops close faster and implement easier than any other system. If a prospect says "QuickBooks" and has multiple locations, they are in your sweet spot.

**The LOC SMS integration is your grocery-specific wedge.** The dedicated Pixis integration appeared in 11 deals with a 64% win rate. This cost-file validation capability doesn't exist in generic AP tools. When the SE demos LOC SMS integration for a grocery prospect, the win rate jumps to nearly double the pipeline average.

**NetSuite is your volume leak.** Fourteen deals, 11 losses. Together with Oracle (3 losses, 0 wins), these ERPs consumed significant sales cycles with near-zero return. Unless you have a specific competitive play against SquareWorks or can engage before the NetSuite contract is signed, these are poor qualification targets.

**The QBD-to-QBO migration is your strongest inbound signal.** NCG-mandated migrations create system-change urgency that compresses deal cycles and increases close rates.

### **ERP-Driven Qualification Framework**

Based on the data, here's how ERP should factor into deal qualification:

**Green Light (High Conversion):** QuickBooks Online, QuickBooks Desktop (migrating to QBO), LOC SMS present — proceed aggressively, these convert at 64%+ when they reach pricing.

**Yellow Light (Qualify Carefully):** Sage 100/300 (requires SE validation of integration), Sage Intacct (confirm they're live, not migrating; watch for native AP competition), FMS (requires FMS team coordination, longer implementation).

**Red Light (Likely Loss):** NetSuite (21% win rate; especially if SquareWorks is in play or prospect is mid-migration), Oracle (0% win rate; migration consumes all organizational bandwidth), PDI-required accounts (no integration exists).

## **Part 3: Connecting the Dots — Why Pipeline Isn't Converting**

The buyer journey and ERP data together tell a clear story about your pipeline-to-MRR conversion problem:

**1\. The Stage 2→3 gap is where your pipeline bleeds out.** Of 82 deals that reach demo, only 45 progress to business case — a 45% attrition rate. This single transition point accounts for more lost revenue than any other factor. The fix isn't better demos — it's making the demo produce the inputs for the business case (invoice volume, AP headcount cost, error rate, EB name).

**2\. The economic buyer isn't hearing the pitch.** In 43 of 68 lost deals (63%), the economic buyer was never engaged on any call. Your AEs are building relationships with AP managers, bookkeepers, and operations directors who love the product but can't sign the check. The fix isn't better messaging — it's a hard gate: "No pricing call without owner/CFO confirmed." Won deals with EB by Call 3 close at 85%.

**3\. You're spending cycles on ERPs where you can't win.** Fourteen lost deals were on NetSuite (11 losses) or Oracle (3 losses) — ERPs with 0–21% win rates. Another 18 deals didn't even document the ERP, suggesting minimal qualification. If you eliminate deals on Red Light ERPs from pipeline, your win rate climbs from 33% to approximately 50%.

**4\. The ROI case isn't being made.** Fifty-six of sixty-eight lost deals (82%) never received any form of ROI model. The won deals that closed fastest all featured the prospect doing the math themselves — because the AE gave them the inputs. Build the ROI model before the pricing call. Superior Grocers' CFO explicitly asked for value justification and never received it. NuCara's CFO was blindsided by pricing without context. Food City's Rick asked for ROI and went to a competitor during 4.5 months of silence.

**5\. Your best segment (independent grocery on QBO with LOC SMS) is underweighted in pipeline.** QBO deals close at 64%. LOC SMS deals close at 64%. The overlap — grocery cooperatives and independents running QBO with LOC SMS — is where product-market fit is strongest, sales cycles are shortest, and the integration story is most compelling. Tilting pipeline generation toward NCG cooperatives, independent grocers on QBO, and LOC SMS shops would materially improve conversion.
