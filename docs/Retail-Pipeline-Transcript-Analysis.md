# **Retail Pipeline Analysis: Call Transcript Insights**

**Analysis Date:** March 24, 2026 **Dataset:** 101 retail deals (33 closed-won, 68 closed-lost) | ~545 call transcripts **Sales Team:** Jett Klintworth, Matt Berney, Samir Regragui, Sade' Santana, Joe Giarmo

---

## **1\. Segment Engagement & Progression**

### **Which segments are most engaged and progressing?**

**Independent & Ethnic Grocery Chains (2–10 stores)** is the strongest-performing segment by a wide margin. Deals like Chavez Supermarket (6 calls, 65-day cycle, $81K ACV), Lunardis (10 calls, 6-week cycle, 301% ROI quantified), Tropicana Supermarkets (7 calls), and Fresh Farms International all closed with strong momentum. These operators feel pain acutely — they're big enough to drown in invoices but too small for enterprise AP systems. They process 500–1,500 invoices per month with skeleton AP staffing, and the CFO or owner is often personally involved in check runs. When the economic buyer is present early, these deals close fast.

**Food Cooperatives** are the second most active segment and close at a strong rate. Feather River Food Coop, GreenStar Food Coop, North Coast Co-Op, and Middlebury Natural Foods Co-Op all closed won. Coops have a unique urgency driver: the NCG-mandated QuickBooks Desktop-to-Online migration forces a system rethink, creating a natural buying window. Their staff are also dramatically overstretched — one GM described doing "seven different jobs all at once." The pain is structural and well-understood by leadership, which shortens the approval process.

**Regional Supermarket Chains (5–15 stores)** engage deeply but take longer to close. Deals like Ball's Food Stores, Karns Foods, McCaffrey's, and Trade Fair closed won, but cycles are longer and require multi-stakeholder navigation across operations, finance, and store management. The complexity of multi-location GL mapping, DSD receiving, and vendor allowance tracking creates strong technical fit — but also means more people need to sign off. When these deals stall (Hugo's, Trig's, Sendik's), it's typically because the economic buyer never engaged.

**Segments that stall or lose:** Pharmacy (NuCara, Astrup Drug, Fruth, Westmeade — all lost), Non-Food Retail (Boot World, Taos Footwear, Bomb Party, Microtech Knives — all lost), and Manufacturing/Distribution (Daybreak Foods, Lusamerica, Virtual Supply — all lost). Pharmacy deals hit pricing walls because lower invoice volumes make per-unit ROI hard to justify. Non-food retail shows curiosity but the pain isn't acute enough to prioritize. Manufacturing gets stuck on integration complexity (Bellwether PO, F&O, custom ERPs) and deprioritizes AP automation behind ERP projects.

## **2\. Most Common Pain Points (Especially Later-Stage Calls)**

Pain points shift as deals progress. Early calls surface broad frustrations with manual processes; later-stage calls reveal the specific, quantifiable problems that justify investment.

### **Universal Pain Points (Mentioned in 90%+ of Deals)**

**Manual invoice processing and GL coding** is the single most consistent pain point across every segment. Prospects describe physically handling paper invoices, handwriting codes, and manually entering data into QuickBooks, ECRS, or proprietary accounting systems. In later-stage calls, this pain gets quantified: Tropicana's bookkeeper spent a full-time role processing invoices across 5 locations. 92nd & Lisbon Sentry calculated $125/week in labor just for AP entry. Feather River's finance manager described staff "doing six different jobs" because there was no automation to offload the work.

**Lack of centralized invoice visibility** appears in virtually every multi-location deal. Store-level staff receive and verify invoices — or don't — with no oversight from the central office. In later-stage calls, prospects connect this to real financial impact: Brooks Grocery discovered $75,000+ in uncaught sales tax overcharges on a single vendor because the only way to see invoices was to "go into the store and actually pull them out of a filing cabinet." Tropicana couldn't compare prices across locations because "it would be going through the manual invoices."

**Payment process fragmentation** is acute in coops and smaller independents. Prospects describe managing ACH, checks, credit cards, and wire transfers through multiple disconnected systems. Feather River's finance manager said she uses "sometimes five different ways to pay people" across QuickBooks, bank portals, Melio, and vendor-specific payment links — creating a reconciliation nightmare.

### **Later-Stage Pain Points (Pricing/Scoping Calls)**

**Decentralized store operations creating execution gaps** surfaces prominently in scoping and pricing calls with multi-location grocers. Pricing coordinators in each store are supposed to enter deals and allowances, but it doesn't happen consistently. Brooks Grocery's COO said it plainly: "I'm afraid that those allowances aren't being put in either." Receiving staff scan invoices without verifying quantities — "It may be scanned in, but it doesn't mean that it always matches. We're still paying that invoice anyway."

**Vendor overcharges and hidden cost leakage** becomes a deciding factor in later conversations. Brooks Grocery found they'd been charged a different price than quoted by Cisco on $1M+ in certified Angus beef purchases. 92nd & Lisbon Sentry was paying a manager $25/hour for AP entry and downgraded to a $13/hour part-timer — trading quality for cost savings. Town & Country acknowledged that "usually if we find a mistake, we're going to find it in another store too."

**Cash flow uncertainty from manual payment tracking** — knowing which checks have cleared, which are outstanding, and what the real bank balance is — gets framed as a management need in pricing discussions. 92nd & Lisbon Sentry maintained a manual Excel check register to track outstanding checks with a 7–14 day clearing window because "each company is different as far as when they want to check their mail."

**Uncaptured vendor allowances and deal credits** becomes a tangible ROI lever in pricing discussions. Multiple grocery prospects acknowledged that store-level staff simply aren't entering allowances. Brooks Grocery's COO said: "the deals are, have to be put in the allowances and things have to be put in at each store by the receiving manager. I'm afraid that those allowances aren't being put in either." This often tips the cost-neutral math that closes deals.

## **3\. Roles That Show Up When Deals Get Serious**

There's a consistent pattern in the buyer composition as deals progress from discovery to close:

### **Early Calls (Discovery / Demo)**

* **AP Manager or Bookkeeper** — the person living the daily pain. Often the initial contact. They validate the workflow fit and describe current-state processes in detail.
* **Operations Director or Store Manager** — evaluates the product against current receiving and vendor management processes. In grocery, this person understands the DSD workflow and back-door controls.

### **Mid-Cycle (Second Demo / Scoping)**

* **IT or Systems Admin** — appears when integration questions arise (QuickBooks connectivity, ECRS/POS data feeds, cost file validation). Eric at GreenStar, the IT contact at Virtual Supply, and the systems team at URM Stores all entered at this stage.
* **Controller or Finance Manager** — in multi-location setups, the controller validates that the GL mapping, entity structure, and payment workflow will work across all locations. Irene at Feather River, Rosa at Tropicana, and Darby at Brooks all served this role.

### **Late Calls (Pricing / Contract)**

* **CFO or Owner** — the economic buyer. In every closed-won deal, the owner or CFO was actively engaged by the pricing call. Tony at Lunardis, Pietro at Sogno Toscano, Felipe at Tropicana, and the ownership group at URM Stores all personally validated pricing.
* **CEO or Multi-Unit Operator** — appears in the largest deals. Hugo's had Doug Driscoll and Dale Ebensteiner (senior leadership). Lola's Market had David Ortega (owner) shielded by gatekeepers — and the deal was lost.

### **The Critical Pattern: Owner/CFO Timing**

In **closed-won deals**, the owner or CFO was actively engaged by Call 3 on average. In **closed-lost deals**, the economic buyer was either never engaged (in roughly 60–70% of cases), engaged too late (after pricing was presented without their input), or overruled by external advisors (accountants, lawyers).

| Owner/CFO Engagement | Won | Lost |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| Engaged by Call 3 | ~24/33 (73%) | ~12/68 (18%) |
| Engaged Call 4+ | ~7/33 (21%) | ~14/68 (21%) |
| Never engaged | ~2/33 (6%) | ~42/68 (62%) |

**When a deal gets serious, you'll see the owner or CFO, an operations/IT person, and typically 3–5 distinct personas across calls.** If by Call 3 you're still only talking to the AP manager or bookkeeper, the deal is likely stalling.

## **4\. Exact Prospect Language**

### **Around Cost and Pricing**

Prospects talk about cost in three distinct ways:

**Sticker shock** — the number lands without ROI context:

* *"It was just the cost of it... was just a number we couldn't reconcile to at that time"* — Rick Elliot, Food City Supermarkets
* *"You're adding another cost, I mean, you're adding a significant cost to it"* — Rick Elliot, Food City Supermarkets
* *"I wish we would have known that you were going to charge 80,000 dollars. If it was doubled or even tripled, I probably wouldn't even have think twice about it, but you're way beyond that"* — CFO, NuCara Pharmacy
* *"The payback on something like that would be... it's difficult to determine how long it would take to recover"* — Rick Elliot, Food City Supermarkets

**Headcount comparison math** — prospects benchmark against what they already pay for labor:

* *"All we have in that department is one person and we're going to have to keep that one person no matter what... you're adding another cost"* — Rick Elliot, Food City Supermarkets
* *"I'm paying him 25 dollars an hour. He's spending four or five hours doing that once a week, that's 125 dollars just to put everything into the account"* — Navi S, 92nd & Lisbon Sentry (chose to hire $13/hr part-timer instead)
* *"I think really this is going to be pricing. I mean, that's going to be the biggest factor especially for our owner to make a decision"* — Town & Country Supermarket

**Price isn't the real blocker** — timing/priority is the actual objection:

* *"I told him... even if it's half price, he's still going to say we're not looking at it"* — Martin, Cermak Fresh Market (champion describing EB rejection)
* *"All the offers and the discounts cause they're incredible... but I just know, yeah, like that's it for him for now"* — Martin, Cermak Fresh Market

### **Around Errors and Manual Processes**

Prospects describe their manual pain with visceral, emotional language:

## **5\. Objection Patterns and Stalled Deals**

### **Primary Objections (Ranked by Frequency)**

**1\. "Not the right time" / Competing priorities (27 of 68 lost deals)**

This is the most common objection and it takes several forms:

* **New store openings or expansion:** Cermak Fresh Market's owner was "completely focused on the new store opening... completely shut off from it." Town & Country was "still in growth mode... expanding into the hardware industry." Hugo's had "other projects taking precedence." In each case, AP automation got sequenced behind capital projects.
* **ERP migration in flight:** Superior Grocers prioritized Oracle implementation. Self Serve Petroleum was mid-migration to Oracle. Virtual Supply had an aggressive F&O timeline. Ottimate got positioned as "after the ERP" instead of "alongside the ERP."
* **Organizational instability:** Swanson's Foods lost an AP team member and the owner disengaged. Murphy's Markets' owner (Pat) was unavailable for a month (Mexico, personal issues). Fiesta Foods went through a 6-month silence that killed all context.

**Typical language:** *"I like to give this the time and attention it deserves, not to put you off again. But I just, it's just not happening right now"* — Town & Country. *"He's just completely like shut off from it unfortunately"* — Martin, Cermak (describing owner).

**2\. Economic buyer never engaged (42 of 68 lost deals)**

This isn't an objection prospects raise — it's a structural failure. The pattern: Ottimate builds enthusiasm with the AP manager or operations contact, presents pricing, and then learns that someone higher (owner, CEO, CFO) needs to approve. That person either never appears or kills the deal without hearing the business case.

Examples:

* Cermak Fresh Market: Martin (champion) had no authority. Mike (owner/EB) was identified but never spoke to the sales team. Martin reported: *"He just basically shot it down and said, oh, this isn't the time right now."*
* Maria Pinto: Alaric acted as gatekeeper. Christina (EB) never appeared. 163-day stall post-demo.
* Hugo's Family Marketplace: Finance decision-maker never engaged across 18 calls over 10 months. EB explicitly deprioritized.
* Vicente Foods: Erika (operator) was the only contact. CFO/EB never identified. Proposal delivered in 9-minute call.

**3\. "We need to see ROI" / No business case built (17 of 68 lost deals)**

Prospects didn't say "your product is too expensive" — they said they couldn't justify the spend internally without a clear ROI model. In 17 deals, no customized ROI analysis was ever delivered to the economic buyer.

* Food City: Rick Elliot explicitly said *"the payback on something like that would be... it's difficult to determine how long it would take to recover"* — and never received quantified math.
* NuCara: CFO experienced sticker shock at $80K. No ROI model tied the cost to recovered labor, eliminated errors, or captured allowances.
* King International: Kushan estimated $400/month; actual pricing was $1,062/month. No value case bridged the gap.
* Lusamerica: Michal (CFO) deprioritized AP automation. External advisors (accountant/lawyers) said "too expensive." No financial case was built to overcome their objection.

**4\. Competitive loss or integration blocker (10 of 68 lost deals)**

When prospects chose a competitor or couldn't technically proceed:

* **PDI integration** — Loop Neighborhood stated "not seriously considering" any non-PDI solution. Hard requirement, not a preference.
* **Bellwether PO** — Daybreak Foods needed PO matching integration that Ottimate couldn't support.
* **Failed prior implementation** — Stewart's Marketplace had a failed first deployment. Owner explicitly blocked re-engagement: *"weren't up for making changes."*
* **Custom app sunk cost** — Sin Lee Food's team had built a custom inventory app. Team consensus favored status quo over switching.
* **Competing vendors** — B&R Autowrecking evaluated Brex, Ramp, Divi alongside Ottimate. CEO was never engaged to validate Ottimate's differentiation.

**5\. Hidden decision-maker veto (5 of 68 lost deals)**

External advisors or unseen stakeholders kill the deal after it looked alive:

* S&K Incorporated: Accountant and lawyers recommended against Ottimate as "too expensive." Sales team never engaged these advisors directly.
* Lusamerica: Same pattern — advisor meeting blocked deal invisibly. Eight-month silence followed.
* Lola's Market: Owner (David Ortega) protected by gatekeepers (Memo, Carlos). Embezzlement history made trust impossible to build remotely. Seven proposals over 3+ months went nowhere.

### **Stall Patterns**

**The silent death:** 14 of 68 lost deals had 5+ month gaps between the last substantive call and the eventual loss. California Fresh Market (9 months), Swanson's Foods (6 months), Hugo's (8 months), Ole City Market (3 months), and Mackenthun's (5 months) all died from inaction rather than active rejection. The pattern: a promising demo or scoping call, a vague next step, and then silence.

**The deferred "yes":** 8 deals used language like "let's revisit after \[event\]" — after the new store opens, after Oracle goes live, after the owner gets back, after we finish year-end. None of these deferred commitments converted.

**The champion who couldn't sell internally:** In approximately 20 lost deals, the operational champion (AP manager, operations director, bookkeeper) was enthusiastic but couldn't get budget approval. They lacked either the authority or the internal business case materials to advocate effectively. Cermak's Martin said it plainly: *"Even if it's half price, he's still going to say we're not looking at it"* — and had nothing to change the owner's mind.

## **6\. Key Strategic Takeaways**

**Independent grocery (2–10 stores) is the beachhead.** This segment has the highest volume of deals, the fastest close cycles when the owner or CFO engages early, and acute pain that maps directly to the product's strengths — invoice capture, cost visibility, vendor payment consolidation. The buying process is simpler (typically owner + bookkeeper, not a 10-person committee).

**Food cooperatives are a secondary sweet spot with a built-in urgency trigger.** The NCG-mandated QuickBooks Desktop-to-Online migration creates a natural buying window. Coops that are migrating systems are already in "change mode" — adding AP automation at the same time is a low-friction sell. Staff overwhelm at coops is extreme and well-understood by leadership.

**Owner/CFO engagement by Call 3 is the single strongest predictor of winning.** Roughly 73% of won deals had the economic buyer engaged by Call 3. 62% of lost deals never engaged the economic buyer at all. This should be a hard qualification gate: if the owner or CFO hasn't been on a call by the third meeting, the deal should be escalated or disqualified.

**The ROI gap is killing deals.** Seventeen lost deals had no customized ROI model delivered. The won deals that closed fastest all had clear math: Lunardis' 301% ROI against a $625K cost baseline, Sentry's $125/week labor quantification, Brooks' $75K+ in uncaught sales tax overcharges. Build the ROI model before the pricing call, not after.

**"Not the right time" is a solvable objection, not a dead end.** Twenty-seven deals were lost to competing priorities — new store openings, ERP migrations, organizational turnover. The counter-positioning needs to be stronger: AP automation reduces the operational strain during expansion, complements (not competes with) ERP go-lives, and becomes harder to implement later when the organization is larger. Cermak's champion explicitly said the owner was focused on the new store — but nobody framed how AP automation would help during that exact transition.

**Hidden advisors (accountants, lawyers) are an unaddressed threat.** At least 5 deals were killed by external advisors who were never identified or engaged by the sales team. S&K Inc and Lusamerica both lost after advisors recommended against the spend. If the prospect mentions an accountant, bookkeeper advisor, or lawyer as part of their decision process, that person needs to be engaged directly — or at minimum, armed with a financial case through the champion.
