# Deal triggers

**Deal triggers**

What causes a prospect to engage with Ottimate

These are the specific events that move a prospect from *knowing they have a problem* to *actually doing something about it*. This isn’t about why they pick Ottimate over a competitor. It’s about what happened in their world that made them start looking in the first place. If we understand these triggers, we can help marketing find the right prospects at the right time, help SDRs time their outreach, and help AEs figure out quickly whether the urgency is real.

| 1\. Staff loss or turnover on the AP team   MOST COMMON    Someone on the AP team quits, retires, or goes on leave, and the manual process that depended on their knowledge falls apart. The rest of the team can’t absorb the work, hiring a replacement takes months, and the cost of specialized AP staff keeps going up. This was the most frequent trigger across our closed-won deals. It showed up in more than half of them. Strongest in: Senior living, hospitality, grocery Typical buyer: Controller, VP of Finance, or CFO who’s dealing with the staffing gap Discovery signal: *“Have you had any turnover on your AP team in the last 12 months? What happened to the workload when that person left?”* |
| :---- |

| 2\. Growth event: acquisitions, new locations, new entities   HIGH IMPACT   A 5-location operator becomes a 12-location operator. A DSO picks up three new practices. A hotel management company wins a new portfolio. Whatever AP process they had at the old scale just doesn’t hold up anymore, and they realize pretty quickly that they can’t hire fast enough to keep up. Strongest in: Hospitality, DSOs, senior living, multi-unit grocery Typical buyer: CFO, VP of Operations, or COO managing the integration Discovery signal: *“Are you adding any new locations or entities in the next 6 to 12 months? How is your current AP process going to handle the additional volume?”* |
| :---- |

| 3\. Audit finding or compliance pressure   EXTERNAL FORCING FUNCTION   An audit turns up missing documentation, GL coding inconsistencies, or gaps in approval trails. Or the board puts out a mandate for stronger financial controls. These external events create urgency that internal frustration alone never could, because the deadline is coming from the outside. Someone told them to fix it, and now they have to. Strongest in: Senior living (Medicare/Medicaid), hospitality (USALI), grocery (margin reporting) Typical buyer: CFO or Controller responding to the board, auditors, or a regulatory body Discovery signal: *“When was your last audit, and did anything come back around AP documentation or approval controls that you’re still working to address?”* |
| :---- |

| 4\. ERP migration or system overhaul   NATURAL ON-RAMP   The company is already moving to Sage Intacct, NetSuite, Dynamics, or another modern ERP. Everything’s being re-evaluated anyway. So the question shifts from “should we automate AP?” to “are we really going to rebuild the manual process on a brand new system?” The switching cost of adding AP automation during a migration is way lower than doing it as a separate project later. Strongest in: All verticals, especially mid-market operators outgrowing QuickBooks or legacy Sage Typical buyer: IT Director or Controller leading the ERP project Discovery signal: *“Are you in the middle of or planning an ERP migration? What’s the timeline, and has anyone talked about how AP will work on the new system?”* |
| :---- |

| 5\. Month-end close breaking point   EMOTIONAL TRIGGER   The controller worked every weekend last month. Close took 12 days instead of 5\. Reconciliation errors forced restatements. This one is less about a single event and more about frustration building up over time until someone finally says “I’m done doing it this way.” It often coincides with a new fiscal year, a new boss, or just the person reaching their limit. The dollar impact is sometimes modest, but the emotional weight behind it is real. Strongest in: All verticals Typical buyer: Controller or AP Manager living through it every month Discovery signal: *“Walk me through the last 48 hours of your most recent month-end close. How many people were involved, and how many of those hours were overtime?”* |
| :---- |

| 6\. Vendor payment friction or margin leakage   FINANCIAL TRIGGER   Late payment penalties piling up. Early-pay discounts being missed. Duplicate payments discovered after the fact. In grocery specifically, vendor credit leakage is a big one: missed promotional credits, unrecovered short ships, pricing discrepancies on invoices that nobody caught. Margins are already thin, and when the CFO finally adds up what’s being left on the table, the conversation starts. Strongest in: Grocery (strongest), hospitality, senior living Typical buyer: CFO, VP of Finance, or Purchasing Director Discovery signal: *“Do you have a sense of how much you’re losing annually to late fees, missed discounts, or vendor billing errors? Is anyone actively tracking that today?”* |
| :---- |

| 7\. New financial leader arrives   CHAMPION-LED   A new Controller, CFO, or VP of Finance joins the company from somewhere that already had AP automation in place. They know what good looks like. They don’t need to be convinced that the category matters. They show up with a mandate to modernize, and they’re already looking for a vendor. These deals tend to move the fastest because the internal champion has both conviction and authority from day one. Strongest in: All verticals Typical buyer: Newly hired Controller or CFO, usually within their first 6 months Discovery signal: *“How long have you been in this role? What did AP automation look like at your previous company?”* |
| :---- |

| 8\. Frustration with their current solution   COMPETITIVE DISPLACEMENT   They already have AP automation (or something close to it), but they’re not happy. Maybe the pricing went up and they can’t justify it anymore. Maybe the service has been declining and support tickets go nowhere. Maybe the product just doesn’t do what they need it to do, whether that’s a specific integration, a vertical capability, or just basic reliability. Whatever the reason, they’re not starting from scratch on the concept of AP automation. They already believe in the category. They’re shopping for something better. Strongest in: All verticals, especially mid-market operators who adopted early and outgrew their first tool Typical buyer: Controller or CFO who’s been living with the shortcomings and finally has budget or mandate to switch Discovery signal: *“What are you using today for AP, and what’s working well? More importantly, what’s not? If you could change one thing about your current setup, what would it be?”* |
| :---- |

# **How often these showed up in our wins**

Here’s how these triggers mapped to the 9 closed-won deals we recently analyzed. Keep in mind that most deals had more than one trigger at play.

| Trigger | Deals | Examples |
| :---- | :---: | :---- |
| Staff loss / turnover | 5 of 9 | Chicken Ranch, McCaffreys, MEP, Gulf Coast, Lunardi’s |
| Growth event | 3 of 9 | Discovery Land, Gulf Coast, Deal 1 |
| Compliance / audit pressure | 3 of 9 | Deal 1, Concord, Discovery Land |
| ERP migration | 2 of 9 | Gulf Coast, Concord |
| Month-end close pain | 4 of 9 | Concord, Lunardi’s, McCaffreys, MEP |
| Vendor payment friction | 2 of 9 | McCaffreys, Lunardi’s |
| New financial leader | 3 of 9 | Who Brew, McCaffreys, MEP |
| Competitor frustration | 3 of 9 | Concord, Discovery Land, McCaffreys |

The most common pairing we saw was staffing pressure combined with month-end close frustration. Competitor frustration tends to pair with growth events or a new leader arriving, since both of those create a natural moment to re-evaluate. It’s rarely just one thing. Usually there’s a primary trigger that gets someone to pick up the phone, and then one or two other pain points come out during discovery that reinforce the urgency.

# **What this means for the rest of the business**

**For Product:** These triggers tell us what problems prospects are trying to solve when they first reach out. Roadmap priorities should reflect the triggers that generate the most pipeline, not just the features that existing customers ask for.

**For Marketing:** Outbound campaigns and content should be timed around trigger events. Job postings for AP roles are a signal of staffing pain. Acquisition announcements signal growth. ERP vendor partnerships signal migration windows. G2 and Capterra review activity can signal competitor frustration. We should be mapping intent data to these triggers instead of just broad "AP automation" keywords.

**For Sales:** Discovery should surface the trigger on the first call. If the prospect can’t explain what changed, what made them start looking now instead of six months ago, the urgency might not be real. For competitive displacement deals, discovery needs to go a layer deeper: is the frustration about price, service, or features? That changes the entire positioning strategy.

## **Persona Notes by Vertical**

**Hospitality (48% win rate, strongest vertical):** The Controller is the dominant persona across all triggers. In multi-property hotel/resort groups, the Regional Controller or Director of Finance owns both the pain and the decision. General Managers are common economic buyers but rarely feel the AP pain directly — they approve spend when the Controller builds the case. In country clubs and golf operations, the title is often Director of Finance or Administrator with GM as the final sign-off.

**Retail (33% win rate):** Multi-unit restaurant and grocery groups show a split: the Controller or Corporate Controller feels the pain, but the Owner/President is often the economic buyer with direct approval authority. In franchise/multi-location retail, the CFO is more likely to be the actor. AP Managers appear more frequently in retail than hospitality as the operational pain owner, making them useful secondary contacts for SDRs to validate the trigger before reaching the Controller.

**Healthcare (32% win rate):** Senior living and DSO (dental) verticals show a distinct pattern: the Controller or VP Finance owns the pain, but organizational complexity (parent companies, PE ownership, management companies) means the economic buyer may be one or two levels above the pain owner. In PE-backed healthcare, the CFO at the management company level must approve, and they may not feel the AP pain at all. SDRs should call the Controller but must immediately map the approval chain.

**Emerging Markets (15% win rate):** The lowest win-rate vertical shows the most persona fragmentation. Deals involve a wider range of titles — from AP Specialists to Presidents — and the path to economic buyer is less clear. The common pattern on losses: a single operational contact engages enthusiastically but lacks authority to push the deal forward. SDRs should qualify for decision-making authority earlier in Emerging Markets than in other verticals.

**CAS (12% win rate):** Client Accounting Services firms are a difficult sell. The pain owner is typically a Manager or Partner, but their business model is built on manual labor — automating AP threatens headcount. The economic buyer (Partner) must be convinced that automation enables scale (more clients) rather than cost-cutting (fewer staff). Only 1 win in the dataset — insufficient data for confident persona mapping.

# Prototype for Surfacing Triggers

# **SDR Persona Playbook — Trigger Discovery by Role**

**Dataset:** 463 deals (162 Won, 301 Lost) | 1,993 call transcripts | March 2026

**How to use this document:** You're on the phone or writing an email. You know the person's title. This document tells you (1) which buying triggers are most likely for that persona, (2) which triggers actually lead to wins when this persona is involved, and (3) the questions to ask to surface those triggers in the first conversation.

---

## **Controller**

**102 deals in dataset | 43% win rate | The \#1 persona across all Ottimate deals**

The Controller is the most common and most productive contact in the dataset. They appear in more won deals than any other title, and their win rate (43%) is well above the 35% baseline. Controllers feel AP pain directly, can articulate the cost, and usually own or influence the budget decision.

### **Triggers to look for (ranked by how often they lead to wins)**

| Priority | Trigger | How often it shows up | Win rate when present |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| 1st | **T7: New financial leader** | 18% of Controller deals | 61% win rate |
| 2nd | **T9: Paper/manual digitization** | 11% of Controller deals | 55% win rate |
| 3rd | **T5: Month-end close pain** | 39% of Controller deals | 50% win rate |
| 4th | **T8: Frustration with current solution** | 85% of Controller deals | 47% win rate |
| 5th | **T4: ERP migration** | 54% of Controller deals | 47% win rate |
| 6th | **T3: Audit/compliance pressure** | 41% of Controller deals | 45% win rate |
| 7th | **T2: Growth event** | 43% of Controller deals | 41% win rate |
| 8th | **T6: Vendor payment friction** | 15% of Controller deals | 40% win rate |
| 9th | **T1: Staff loss/turnover** | 45% of Controller deals | 39% win rate |

### **Discovery questions for Controllers**

**Open with month-end (their world revolves around the close):**

* "How did last month's close go? How many days does it typically take your team?"  
* "What's the most tedious part of your close process right now?"  
* "Are there weekends or late nights involved around close — or has the team gotten it under control?"

**Surface ERP and system context (Controllers always know what's changing):**

* "What accounting system are you on? Any plans to change that in the next 12 months?"  
* "If you are moving to a new ERP — are you evaluating your AP workflow as part of that, or handling it separately?"

**Probe for current solution frustration (85% of Controller deals have this):**

* "How are you handling AP today — is there a system in place, or is it more manual?"  
* "If you have an AP tool, what's working and what isn't?"  
* "What would your AP team say if I asked them what they'd change about the current process?"

**Check for staffing pressure:**

* "How big is your AP team right now? Has that changed in the last year?"  
* "If someone on the AP team called in sick for a week, what would happen?"

**Test for compliance/audit exposure:**

* "When was your last audit? Did anything come up on the AP or approval workflow side?"  
* "How do you track approvals today — is there a documented trail, or is it more informal?"

**Qualify for decision authority (critical — Controllers may or may not own the budget):**

* "If this looked like a fit, who besides you would need to weigh in on a decision like this?"  
* "Is there a budget already allocated for AP improvements, or would this need to go through a new approval?"

## **CFO**

**69 deals in dataset | 36% win rate | Strategic buyer, not always the pain owner**

CFOs appear in more lost deals than won deals. That's not because CFOs are bad contacts — it's because deals that reach the CFO without a strong internal champion tend to stall. The CFO approves spend but rarely feels AP pain daily. The highest-converting triggers when a CFO is involved are growth events and month-end pain — things with clear financial impact they care about.

### **Triggers to look for**

| Priority | Trigger | How often it shows up | Win rate when present |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| 1st | **T9: Paper/manual digitization** | 12% of CFO deals | 62% win rate |
| 2nd | **T7: New financial leader** | 17% of CFO deals | 50% win rate |
| 3rd | **T5: Month-end close pain** | 35% of CFO deals | 42% win rate |
| 4th | **T1: Staff loss/turnover** | 55% of CFO deals | 39% win rate |
| 5th | **T8: Frustration with current solution** | 81% of CFO deals | 38% win rate |
| 6th | **T6: Vendor payment friction** | 23% of CFO deals | 38% win rate |
| 7th | **T4: ERP migration** | 43% of CFO deals | 37% win rate |
| 8th | **T2: Growth event** | 57% of CFO deals | 36% win rate |
| 9th | **T3: Audit/compliance** | 39% of CFO deals | 26% win rate |

### **Discovery questions for CFOs**

**Lead with strategic, not operational (CFOs think in dollars and headcount, not invoices):**

* "As you look at the finance team over the next year, where are you trying to get more efficient?"  
* "What's your biggest operational cost concern on the back-office side right now?"  
* "Are there areas where you're still relying on manual processes that you'd like to automate?"

**Surface growth and scaling pain (this is what CFOs act on):**

* "I noticed you've added \[locations/entities/acquisitions\] recently — how has that affected the accounting workload?"  
* "As you scale, are you planning to grow the accounting team proportionally, or are you looking at technology to absorb the volume?"

**Test for month-end visibility (CFOs care about reporting speed, not data entry):**

* "How quickly after month-end do you have financials ready? Is that timeline where you want it?"  
* "Is the close process a bottleneck for your board reporting or investor updates?"

**Probe for vendor payment impact (speak their language — margin and cash flow):**

* "Are you capturing all your early-pay discounts? What's that worth annually?"  
* "Have duplicate payments or late fees been an issue, or do you have that locked down?"

**Qualify whether they're the right person OR whether you need the Controller:**

* "Are you the one evaluating AP tools directly, or is someone on your team leading that?"  
* "Who on your team is closest to the day-to-day AP process?"

**21 deals in dataset | 71% win rate | The hidden gem — highest win rate of any persona**

Directors of Finance appear in fewer deals than Controllers or CFOs, but when they're involved, the win rate is 71%. This title is most common in hospitality (multi-property hotel groups, country clubs) where it's effectively the senior finance person on-property. They feel the pain directly AND have budget influence — the ideal combination.

### **Triggers to look for**

| Priority | Trigger | How often it shows up | Win rate when present |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| 1st | **T7: New financial leader** | 10% of DoF deals | 100% win rate |
| 2nd | **T2: Growth event** | 38% of DoF deals | 88% win rate |
| 3rd | **T5: Month-end close pain** | 33% of DoF deals | 86% win rate |
| 4th | **T6: Vendor payment friction** | 24% of DoF deals | 80% win rate |
| 5th | **T8: Frustration with current solution** | 76% of DoF deals | 75% win rate |
| 6th | **T3: Audit/compliance** | 52% of DoF deals | 73% win rate |
| 7th | **T9: Paper/manual digitization** | 33% of DoF deals | 71% win rate |
| 8th | **T4: ERP migration** | 90% of DoF deals | 68% win rate |
| 9th | **T1: Staff loss/turnover** | 43% of DoF deals | 56% win rate |

### **Discovery questions for Directors of Finance**

**These contacts are usually hands-on — go direct to their operational pain:**

* "Walk me through how an invoice gets from the mailbox to your accounting system today."  
* "How much of your week is spent on AP versus the financial analysis and reporting you were hired to do?"  
* "What's the approval process look like — are you chasing people down for sign-offs?"

**Probe for multi-property complexity (common in hospitality):**

* "How many properties or entities are you managing the finances for?"  
* "Do you have to reconcile across entities, or is each property standalone?"  
* "When a vendor invoice needs to be split across locations, how does that work today?"

**Test for ERP context (90% of DoF deals involve an ERP):**

* "What's your accounting system? Is that working well, or are there changes coming?"  
* "Are you on Jonas/R365/Sage? How does your AP feed into that today?"

## **General Manager**

**13 deals in dataset | 54% win rate | Economic buyer in hospitality, not pain owner**

General Managers rarely feel AP pain directly. They show up as the economic buyer or final approver — the person the Controller needs sign-off from. When a GM is involved in an Ottimate deal, growth events convert best (80% win rate). The GM cares about operations and property performance, not invoice processing.

### **Triggers to look for**

| Priority | Trigger | How often it shows up | Win rate when present |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| 1st | **T2: Growth event** | 38% of GM deals | 80% win rate |
| 2nd | **T3: Audit/compliance** | 38% of GM deals | 60% win rate |
| 3rd | **T4: ERP migration** | 38% of GM deals | 60% win rate |
| 4th | **T1: Staff loss/turnover** | 31% of GM deals | 50% win rate |
| 5th | **T8: Frustration with current solution** | 38% of GM deals | 40% win rate |
| 6th | **T5: Month-end close pain** | 31% of GM deals | 25% win rate |

### **Discovery questions for General Managers**

**Frame it as operational efficiency, not AP mechanics:**

* "How's the back-office operation keeping up with the property's growth?"  
* "Is the finance team staffed where you need them, or are they stretched?"  
* "When you need financial data — weekly P\&L, vendor spend — how quickly can your team get it to you?"

**Surface the Controller as the internal champion (your real goal with a GM):**

* "Who on your team handles the AP and accounting day-to-day? I'd love to understand their workflow."  
* "Has your Controller or Director of Finance flagged any concerns about the invoice process?"

## **AP Manager**

**13 deals in dataset | 15% win rate | Feels the pain acutely but can't buy**

AP Managers are the most dangerous persona to mistake for a buyer. They feel the trigger more than anyone — they live in the broken process every day. But deals where the AP Manager is the primary contact close at only 15%. The AP Manager is useful for validating that a trigger exists and gathering intel. They are not the person to build a deal around.

### **Triggers to look for**

| Priority | Trigger | How often it shows up | Win rate when present |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| 1st | **T1: Staff loss/turnover** | 46% of AP Mgr deals | 33% win rate |
| 2nd | **T4: ERP migration** | 31% of AP Mgr deals | 25% win rate |
| 3rd | **T8: Frustration with current solution** | 85% of AP Mgr deals | 18% win rate |
| 4th | **T2: Growth event** | 46% of AP Mgr deals | 17% win rate |

### **Discovery questions for AP Managers**

**Validate the pain (this is what they're good for):**

* "How many invoices are you processing per month? Has that been going up?"  
* "What's the most frustrating part of your day-to-day process?"  
* "What happens when someone on your team is out — can anyone else cover?"

## **Warning Signs: When to Pivot**

The data shows clear patterns for when a persona contact won't convert on its own. Watch for these signals and act accordingly:

**If you're talking to an AP Manager or AP Clerk and they're enthusiastic but say "I'd need to run this up the chain"** — you don't have a deal, you have a scout. Ask: "Can we set up a quick call with your Controller to get their perspective?" Don't invest more time at this level until you have a path to the decision-maker.

**If you're talking to a CFO and they say "sounds interesting, let me have my Controller look into it"** — that's actually good. The CFO just gave you permission to call the Controller and drop the CFO's name. Do it that day.

**If you're talking to a Controller and they say "I'd need to get my GM/owner to approve"** — map the approval process immediately. Ask: "What would your GM need to see to say yes? Can we build that case together?" The Controller is your champion; help them sell internally.

**If you're talking to anyone and there's no trigger — no pain, no event, no urgency** — this is not a deal right now. Note it, set a 90-day follow-up, and move on. Deals without triggers close at 21%.
