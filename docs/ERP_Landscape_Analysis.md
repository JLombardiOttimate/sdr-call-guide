# ERP Landscape Analysis: Healthcare Prospects

**Analysis Date:** March 26, 2026
**Dataset:** 49 healthcare deals (15 closed-won, 34 closed-lost) | ~254 call transcripts
**Territory:** Healthcare sector (Senior Living, Dental, Community Health Centers, Behavioral Health, others)
**Methodology:** ERP mentions extracted via full-text search across all deal analysis files and validated against individual call transcripts. ERPs (systems of record) are classified separately from AP automation/payment tools (competitors Ottimate displaces).

---

## Executive Summary

The healthcare mid-market runs on Sage. Sage Intacct and Sage 50 together appear in 20–23 of 49 deals, making the Sage ecosystem the dominant ERP across every sub-vertical Ottimate sells into. QuickBooks is the second most common system at 8–10 deals, followed by healthcare-specific platforms (PCC/PointClickCare at 4 deals, MatrixCare at 3). Beyond these, the ERP landscape fragments into systems where Ottimate has a 0% win rate — NetSuite (5), Dynamics (4), Acumatica (3), Yardi (2), Workday (2), and MIP (1).

The most important finding isn't which ERP prospects use — it's how ERP status determines deal outcomes. Seventeen lost deals were on ERPs where Ottimate has never won (NetSuite, Dynamics, Acumatica, Yardi, Workday, MIP). Additional deals stalled because the prospect was mid-ERP-migration on otherwise viable systems. Combined, roughly 59% of lost deals were either structurally unwinnable based on ERP or timing-blocked by migration. If you qualify pipeline by ERP on Call 1, your effective win rate on qualified deals climbs from 31% to well above 50%.

This report covers ERPs (systems of record) separately from AP automation and payment tools (which Ottimate competes with or displaces). These are fundamentally different categories — Ottimate integrates with ERPs but replaces AP tools.

### Sage Intacct — The Volume Play

**Prevalence:** 15–18 of 49 deals (~35%) — by far the most common ERP in the pipeline, both as the current system and as the ERP prospects are migrating toward.

**Where it shows up:** Senior living (St. Croix Health, Silverpoint, Gulf Coast migrating to it), community health centers (Access Health Louisiana), multi-specialty medical (GastroMed), and dental DSOs (Platinum Dental). Sage Intacct is the default mid-market cloud ERP choice across healthcare.

**Win dynamics:** Ottimate's Intacct integration is mature and was cited positively in multiple closed-won deals. Access Health Louisiana (22-day close) ran on Intacct with complex 17-way allocation splits — Ottimate's allocation engine was the differentiator. At Silverpoint, the integration was straightforward and not a friction point. Seven won deals ran Sage Intacct.

**Loss dynamics:** Five or more deals stalled specifically because the prospect was mid-migration to Intacct from a legacy system (Sage 50, Great Plains, or another platform). Providence Life Services said to "revisit in 6–8 months" post-go-live. Desert Recovery Services deferred pending Sage implementation. The pattern is consistent: prospects in the first 0–6 months of an Intacct migration won't evaluate a point solution on top of it.

**The risk within the risk:** Sage Intacct has its own native AP module. While it's generic and lacks healthcare-specific allocation logic, it's "good enough" for some prospects — especially those who just went through a painful ERP migration and don't want another vendor. This didn't surface as a direct loss reason in the data, but it's the likely explanation for several deals that went quiet after the prospect went live on Intacct.

**Qualification guidance:** Confirm on Call 1: "Are you live on Intacct today, or are you in the process of migrating?" If live, proceed — this is your highest-volume ERP target. If migrating, establish a re-engagement date 60 days post-go-live rather than investing further cycles now.

### Sage 50 — The Sweet Spot

**Prevalence:** 4–5 deals, predominantly in senior living / LTC.

**Why it converts at 100%:** Sage 50 (formerly Peachtree) is a desktop accounting application with no native multi-entity AP automation, no approval workflows, and no consolidation capability. Organizations running Sage 50 across multiple facilities — Gulf Coast LTC had 13 separate instances — feel every limitation viscerally. There is no "good enough" native module to compete with. Ottimate is the obvious answer.

Gulf Coast's BJ Bergeron described the pain directly: there's no way to consolidate all 13 entities within their current Sage setup. The manual workaround was Excel spreadsheets, weekly check runs consuming 1–2 full days, and handwritten GL codes on paper invoices.

**Segment context:** Sage 50 shops are typically mid-sized senior living operators (5–20 facilities) that haven't yet migrated to Intacct or another cloud ERP. Many are in the process of considering that migration — which creates a dual opportunity: sell Ottimate as AP automation now, and position it as a tool that will carry forward when they eventually move to Intacct.

**Qualification guidance:** If a prospect says "Sage 50" and has multiple locations, this is your highest-priority lead. Every deal in this dataset that matched that profile closed.

### QuickBooks — High Volume, Inconsistent Conversion

**Prevalence:** 8–10 deals — the second most common ERP in the pipeline after Sage Intacct.

**Where it shows up:** Multi-specialty medical (GastroMed with 4 QB Enterprise instances, Cor Medical Centers with 18 QB instances across 14+ locations), single-entity organizations (Elev8 Center NJ), dental (Diamond Braces), biotech (Lantern Pharma), and others (Preston County). QB spans virtually every healthcare sub-vertical.

**Win dynamics:** Elev8 Center NJ ran QuickBooks and closed quickly (33 days). The QB approval add-on had already failed them — they tried to use it for invoice approvals and found it inadequate, which validated the need for external AP automation before the first demo. GastroMed closed because Ottimate's allocation engine solved the multi-instance QB complexity that no competitor could handle.

**Loss dynamics:** The ~30% win rate reflects a mix of deal types. Single-entity QB shops often don't have enough pain or budget to justify the investment. Several QB deals (Diamond Braces, Cor Medical, Lantern Pharma) were lost to champion power gaps, competing priorities, or ERP migration plans rather than to QB-specific issues. Multi-instance QB shops have acute pain comparable to Sage 50 multi-entity setups, but they're a subset of the total QB pipeline.

**Qualification guidance:** Qualify on invoice volume and multi-location complexity. Multi-instance QB shops (like GastroMed and Cor Medical) are strong targets — the consolidation pain is real. Single-entity QBO shops are good for pipeline velocity but carry lower ACV and less consistent conversion. Ask: "How many QB instances are you running?" The answer determines priority.

### PCC/PointClickCare — Healthcare-Specific, CFO-Dependent

**Prevalence:** 4 deals, all in senior living / skilled nursing.

**Where it shows up:** St. Dominic Village (closed-won), Wesley Manor (closed-won), Trinity Woods (closed-lost), and St. Joseph's Elder Services (closed-lost). PCC is a healthcare-specific clinical and financial platform dominant in skilled nursing and senior living communities.

**Win dynamics:** The 50% win rate tracks CFO engagement perfectly. At St. Dominic Village, CFO Daniel Wagner was engaged from Call 1, did the FTE math himself ("Her assistant makes $55,000 a year. So this is a huge pickup"), and signed within the week. At Wesley Manor, CFO Mark Stephens personally validated pricing. In both won deals, the CFO was the champion.

**Loss dynamics:** Trinity Woods and St. Joseph's Elder Services both stalled on champion power gaps — the operational team was engaged but the economic buyer either didn't appear or deferred. The ERP itself wasn't the barrier; the buyer journey dynamics were.

**Strategic opportunity:** PCC integration capability is a relevant qualifier in the senior living vertical. The platform is widely used in skilled nursing, and Ottimate's ability to connect to it differentiates against competitors who don't support healthcare-specific EMR/financial systems.

**Qualification guidance:** If a prospect mentions PCC/PointClickCare, confirm CFO engagement early. The ERP is viable — the deal outcome depends entirely on whether the economic buyer is in the room.

### MatrixCare — The Healthcare Differentiator

**Prevalence:** 3 deals, all in senior living. Two closed (Covenant Village, Progressive Quality Care), one lost (1 deal).

**Why it matters:** MatrixCare is a healthcare-specific ERP/EMR used primarily in senior living and long-term care. It handles clinical operations, billing, and financials. The critical point: competing AP automation tools (Bill.com, Coupa, AvidXchange) do not natively integrate with MatrixCare. Ottimate does.

At Covenant Village, Jessica Carlyle was manually double-entering invoice data from Square Nine into MatrixCare — 25 hours per week of re-coding. Ottimate's MatrixCare integration eliminated that entirely. At Progressive Quality Care, MatrixCare Enterprise Financials was the core system, and Ottimate's ability to connect to it was a prerequisite.

**Strategic opportunity:** MatrixCare is an underexploited differentiator. If Ottimate can build a MatrixCare-specific go-to-market motion — case studies, integration documentation, presence at MatrixCare user conferences, co-marketing with MatrixCare's sales team — this becomes a defensible niche that competitors can't easily replicate.

**Qualification guidance:** If a prospect mentions MatrixCare, the deal is likely senior living, the integration is a differentiator, and the win rate is high. Prioritize these.

### NetSuite — The Deal-Killer

**Prevalence:** 5 deals, all closed-lost. **0% win rate.**

**What happens:** Vitana Pediatric Orthodontic Partners was the clearest example. They were a PE-backed DSO evaluating Ottimate while simultaneously signing a NetSuite contract. Once NetSuite went live, they stopped the Ottimate evaluation entirely because NetSuite's native AP module — bundled into the ERP licensing — was "good enough." The Ottimate team engaged for 9 months without resolution.

Lantern Pharma was also migrating to NetSuite — same pattern: the AP evaluation was sequenced as "after ERP goes live," and once live, the native module won by default. Libertana runs NetSuite as their core ERP (with CorePay for payments) and the deal died on vendor onboarding workflow friction. Blue Ridge Hospice uses NetSuite with a multi-subsidiary structure and the deal died on budget constraints and HIPAA compliance roadmap gaps. Charlotte Behavioral Health was evaluating NetSuite vs. Business Central as part of their ERP selection.

**Why it's structural:** PE-backed organizations (especially DSOs) often purchase NetSuite with bundled AP/payments as part of a platform standardization play. The AP decision is made at the PE level, not the individual portfolio company level. By the time Ottimate is talking to the local finance team, the ERP contract (with bundled AP) is already signed.

**Qualification guidance:** Ask on Call 1: "Are you evaluating or implementing NetSuite?" If yes, and especially if they're PE-backed, this deal has a near-zero probability of closing. The only viable play is positioning Ottimate before the NetSuite contract is signed — which requires engaging at the PE fund level, not the portfolio company level.

### Microsoft Dynamics (Business Central / GP / NAV) — Partner-Controlled

**Prevalence:** 4 deals, all closed-lost. **0% win rate.**

**What happens:** Charlotte Behavioral Health ran Business Central and chose Continia — a Dynamics-native AP automation solution. The critical detail: Continia was recommended by Opal, Charlotte's Microsoft ERP implementation partner. Ottimate never had a chance to make the case directly to the economic buyer because the trusted advisor (the ERP partner) had already steered the decision.

Providence Life Services was migrating from Great Plains to Intacct — which is actually a positive signal for Ottimate once the migration completes, but the deal was lost during the migration window. Bradford Health Services runs Great Plains.

**Why it's structural:** In the Microsoft ecosystem, ERP implementation partners (like Opal) have outsized influence over AP tool selection. They recommend solutions that integrate natively with Dynamics, and Continia is purpose-built for that. Ottimate is competing against a trusted advisor relationship, not just a product.

**Qualification guidance:** If a prospect runs Dynamics BC/GP and their ERP partner has recommended a competing AP solution, deprioritize. The only counter is to engage the prospect before the ERP partner makes their recommendation — which means getting upstream in the buying process.

### Acumatica — The API Gap

**Prevalence:** 3 deals, all closed-lost. **0% win rate.**

**What happens:** Westminster Communities ran Acumatica and the deal died because Ottimate couldn't support PO ingestion through Acumatica's API. Eden Hill was also an Acumatica shop with similar challenges. The API limitations with Acumatica represent a real product gap — prospects need two-way data flow (invoices out, POs in), and Ottimate's Acumatica integration doesn't support it.

**Qualification guidance:** Until the Acumatica integration supports PO ingestion, these deals are high-risk. Qualify on whether PO matching is a requirement. If it is, this is a product gap that sales can't overcome.

---

### Yardi — Property Management Crossover

**Prevalence:** 2 deals (The Pointes, Epoch Senior Living), both closed-lost. **0% win rate.**

**What happens:** Yardi is a property management ERP that also serves senior living communities with residential/property components. Both deals sat at the intersection of healthcare and property management, and neither progressed past early stages. Yardi's native AP capabilities within its property management suite, combined with the fact that these organizations' financial workflows are structured around property/unit management rather than healthcare-style entity management, make them a poor fit.

**Qualification guidance:** If a prospect mentions Yardi as their primary system, they're likely operating in a property management paradigm. Unless they have a clear, healthcare-specific AP pain that Yardi doesn't solve, deprioritize.

---

### Workday — Wrong Segment

**Prevalence:** 2 deals, both closed-lost. **0% win rate.**

**Why:** Workday is an enterprise-grade cloud ERP focused primarily on HCM (Human Capital Management). Organizations on Workday are typically larger than Ottimate's mid-market sweet spot. The AP module within Workday, while not best-in-class, is part of a broader enterprise platform decision. These are not Ottimate's target buyers.

**Qualification guidance:** Workday signals enterprise complexity and budget. Unless the prospect has a specific, acute AP pain that Workday's native module can't address, these deals are unlikely to close.

---

### MIP (Abila / Community Brands) — Nonprofit ERP

**Prevalence:** 1 deal (Children's Home Network), closed-lost. **0% win rate.**

**What happened:** The new CFO deprioritized the AP automation evaluation because the organization was exploring a full ERP replacement (Oracle/SAP) rather than bolt-on solutions. MIP is a nonprofit-specific ERP, and the prospect's needs were broader than AP automation.

**Qualification guidance:** MIP signals a nonprofit organization that may be undergoing broader financial system transformation. If the prospect is evaluating full ERP replacement, AP automation is unlikely to be prioritized.

These are the tools that sit on top of the ERP and handle invoice capture, approval workflows, payment execution, or some combination. Ottimate doesn't integrate with these — it replaces them.

### Key Competitive Patterns

**Ottimate wins consistently when displacing Bill.com, Paymerang, Beanworks, or Square Nine.** These tools have clear capability gaps that prospects already feel — Bill.com can't handle multi-location GL mapping at scale, Paymerang is payment-only without AP automation, Beanworks lacks payment execution, and Square Nine is document management rather than AP workflow. When a prospect is on one of these tools and hits a ceiling, Ottimate is the natural upgrade path.

**The one exception:** Bill.com won Affinity Home Hospice on invoice capture speed. Ottimate's 8–24 hour processing window was a dealbreaker for a hospice operation that needed real-time OCR. This is a product gap in speed-sensitive segments.

**Ottimate loses to unified platforms (Coupa) and ERP-native solutions (Continia, NetSuite AP).** These losses happen when the buying decision shifts from "best AP tool" to "fewest vendors" or "deepest ERP integration." Coupa won Anthem Memory Care after the CEO overrode the Controller's preference for Ottimate — the CEO wanted a single platform. Continia won Charlotte Behavioral Health because the ERP implementation partner recommended it as part of the Microsoft ecosystem.

**The competitive counter:** Ottimate's advantage over unified platforms is healthcare specialization (allocation logic, MatrixCare/PCC integration, multi-entity support), faster time-to-value (4–6 weeks vs. 12–16 weeks for Coupa), and lower total cost. But this message only works if it reaches the economic buyer — when it's filtered through a controller or AP manager, the "fewer vendors" argument wins.

## Part 4: ERP-Driven Qualification Framework

### Green Light — High Conversion (Proceed Aggressively)

**Sage 50 + Multi-Location:** 100% win rate in this dataset. No native AP automation, acute consolidation pain, and prospects self-select for Ottimate's value prop. If they say "Sage 50" and have 5+ locations, this is the highest-priority lead in your pipeline.

**MatrixCare:** 67% win rate. Healthcare-specific differentiator. No competing AP tools integrate natively. Build case studies here.

**PCC/PointClickCare:** 50% win rate. Wins track CFO engagement perfectly — when the economic buyer is in the room, these close. PCC is ubiquitous in skilled nursing, and integration capability differentiates Ottimate.

### Yellow Light — Qualify Carefully

**Sage Intacct (Live):** ~37% win rate overall, but significantly higher when the prospect is already live on Intacct (vs. migrating to it). Confirm migration status on Call 1. If live, this is your highest-volume ERP target.

**Sage Intacct (Migrating):** Park the deal. Establish a re-engagement date 60 days post-go-live. Do not invest sales cycles during the migration window — these deals defer 100% of the time.

**QuickBooks (Multi-Instance):** ~30% win rate overall, but multi-instance QB shops (like GastroMed with 4 instances, Cor Medical with 18) have pain comparable to Sage 50 and convert well. Single-entity QB shops are smaller and less consistent. Ask how many instances they run.

**Bill.com Incumbent:** High win rate when the prospect has outgrown Bill.com (multi-location GL mapping, approval workflows, compliance). Qualify on what's broken — if it's just "we want something better," that's strong. If the pain is invoice capture speed, Bill.com may defend.

### Red Light — Likely Loss (Deprioritize)

**NetSuite (Any Status):** 0% win rate across 5 deals. Native AP module is bundled. PE-backed organizations pre-purchase. The only play is engaging before the NetSuite contract is signed, ideally at the PE fund level.

**Microsoft Dynamics BC/GP (With ERP Partner Involved):** 0% win rate across 4 deals. Continia owns this ecosystem through partner relationships. If the ERP partner has already recommended a solution, the deal is likely lost.

**Acumatica (PO Matching Required):** 0% win rate across 3 deals. Product gap on PO ingestion. Until this is resolved, these deals are high-risk.

**Yardi:** 0% win rate across 2 deals. Property management crossover — these organizations operate in a property/unit paradigm rather than healthcare entity management. Wrong segment for Ottimate's current capabilities.

**Workday:** 0% win rate across 2 deals. Enterprise-grade — these are organizations above Ottimate's mid-market sweet spot. The AP module is part of a broader enterprise platform decision.

**MIP:** 0% win rate (1 deal). Nonprofit ERP — signals broader financial system transformation rather than point-solution AP need.

## Part 5: Strategic Implications

### The Pipeline Math

If you apply ERP-based qualification to your current dataset:

- **17 of 34 lost deals (50%)** were on ERPs with 0% win rates — NetSuite (5), Dynamics (4), Acumatica (3), Yardi (2), Workday (2), MIP (1).
- An additional 5+ deals were lost to mid-ERP-migration timing on otherwise viable ERPs (primarily Sage Intacct).
- Combined, roughly **20 of 34 lost deals (59%)** were fundamentally unqualifiable or severely timing-blocked.
- Removing them from the denominator, your **effective win rate on qualified deals climbs from 31% to well above 50%**.
- The issue isn't pipeline generation — it's pipeline quality. You're building pipeline on ERPs where you can't win.

### The Sage Opportunity

Sage (Intacct + 50 combined) represents approximately 40–45% of your total deal volume and contains your highest-converting segments. A Sage-first healthcare GTM strategy — co-marketing with Sage, presence at Sage Intacct Advantage, Sage 50 migration partnerships, and Sage-specific case studies — would concentrate resources on the ERP ecosystem where you have the strongest product-market fit. Sage Intacct alone at ~37% win rate isn't spectacular, but it's your volume play, and the win rate climbs substantially when you exclude mid-migration deals.

### The Healthcare-Specific ERP Niche

MatrixCare (67% win rate) and PCC/PointClickCare (50% win rate) together represent a defensible niche. These are healthcare-specific platforms where competing AP tools don't have native integrations. A targeted play in this niche — even just 3–4 case studies, integration documentation, and presence at AHCA/LeadingAge and MatrixCare user conferences — could create an uncontested category position. Combined, these two ERPs appeared in 7 deals with 4 wins, a 57% win rate that outperforms every other ERP except Sage 50.

### The QuickBooks Opportunity

QuickBooks is underperforming at ~30% win rate despite being the second most common ERP. The opportunity is to segment QB prospects more aggressively: multi-instance QB shops (3+ instances) should be treated as Green Light targets, while single-entity QB shops should be qualified harder on invoice volume and budget authority. The pain in multi-instance QB environments is as acute as Sage 50 — the challenge is that single-entity QB shops dilute the overall conversion rate.

### The 0% ERP Problem

Seventeen deals were lost across six ERPs with 0 wins. This isn't a messaging problem — it's a structural disadvantage (native AP modules, partner ecosystem control, PE-level purchasing, API gaps, wrong-segment targeting). The honest strategic choice is to either invest in building competitive integrations for select high-volume 0% ERPs (NetSuite is the most impactful given 5 deals) or to explicitly de-prioritize all six in qualification criteria. The worst option is continuing to invest sales cycles in deals that won't close.

### Call 1 ERP Qualification Script

Based on this data, every first call should include these questions:

1. "What accounting system / ERP are you on today?" — Determines Green/Yellow/Red classification
2. "Are you live on it, or in the process of migrating?" — Catches the migration timing trap
3. "Is there an ERP implementation partner involved?" — Identifies Dynamics/Continia risk
4. "Are you backed by a PE fund or management company that standardizes systems?" — Identifies NetSuite/platform bundling risk
5. "Do you currently have any AP automation or payment tools in place?" — Separates greenfield (67% win rate) from displacement (27% win rate)

If the answers to questions 1–4 point to a Red Light ERP, the deal should be deprioritized immediately — regardless of how engaged the prospect seems. The data shows these deals consume months of sales cycles and close at 0%.
