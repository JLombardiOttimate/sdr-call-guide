## **Deliverable 3: Strategic Narrative**

### **This is a greenfield market, not a displacement market.**

The single most important finding in this analysis is that 90% of retail prospects have no AP automation whatsoever. They process invoices by hand — paper, email, spreadsheets, basic ERP data entry. Ottimate is not competing against other AP automation vendors in this vertical; it is competing against the status quo. Of 32 closed-won deals, not a single one involved displacing an existing AP automation tool. Every won deal was a net-new automation sale to a prospect running a fully manual process. Of 68 closed-lost deals, only 5 (7%) were actually lost to a named competitor. The other 63 (91% including 6 where a competitor was mentioned but not chosen) were lost to inaction, timing, budget constraints, or internal politics — all forms of "the prospect decided to keep doing what they've always done." The retail mid-market has not yet been penetrated by AP automation at any meaningful scale. This is a market creation challenge, not a market share challenge.

### **Bill.com is the most visible competitor but rarely wins head-to-head.**

Bill.com appeared in 7 of the 17 competitive deals — making it the most frequently encountered AP automation tool in the retail pipeline. But the competitive dynamic is nuanced. In the 3 won deals where Bill.com was present (Balboa, World Foods, Herbology), the prospects had evaluated or heard of Bill.com but chose Ottimate. In the 4 lost deals where Bill.com was present (California Fresh, Clearlight, Verlo, Norwex), three were incumbents where the prospect already had Bill.com and the deal was lost to inaction — not because they chose Bill.com over Ottimate, but because they didn't move at all. Only Norwex explicitly chose/stayed with Bill.com. The real competitive threat isn't a specific vendor — it's ERP-native AP embedding (SquareWorks won Astrup Drug by being "embedded in NetSuite"; Continia was evaluated alongside Ottimate at Mirasco in the Dynamics ecosystem), incumbent inertia in the rare cases where automation exists, and fintech spend management tools (Brex/Ramp/Divi) that solve adjacent problems. Traditional AP automation vendors (AvidXchange, Coupa, MineralTree, Medius, Basware, Esker, Transcepta) appeared in zero retail deals — they have no presence in this market segment.

### **The "aware but never adopted" cohort barely exists — and that tells us the market isn't ready, it's unaware.**

Only 2 of 100 deals (2%) showed evidence of prior AP automation evaluation — and both were won deals (Balboa and World Foods had looked at Bill.com before). Zero lost deals showed prior evaluation. This means the vast majority of retail prospects haven't even considered AP automation before Ottimate reaches them. They're not rejecting it after evaluation; they don't know it exists, or they don't believe it applies to them. The implication for go-to-market: competitive battlecards and feature comparison sheets are nearly irrelevant in retail. What matters is educational content that builds awareness of the category, ROI tools that quantify the cost of manual processes (since prospects haven't done this math themselves), and customer references from businesses that look exactly like the prospect. The sales motion is missionary, not competitive. The 91% of lost deals that died to inaction confirm this — the failure mode isn't "they chose someone else," it's "they chose to do nothing." Solving that requires a fundamentally different playbook than winning competitive evaluations: shorter time-to-value demonstrations, faster proof of ROI, and earlier economic buyer engagement so the person with budget authority understands why this is worth the disruption.
