## Deliverable 3: Strategic Narrative

### This is predominantly a greenfield market — but the losses are not.

The most striking pattern in this data is the asymmetry between wins and losses on the AP automation status dimension. **Ten of fifteen closed-won deals (67%) came from prospects with no existing AP automation** — organizations running fully manual, paper-based AP processes on top of their ERP. Only four wins came from displacing an incumbent tool. Meanwhile, the loss pool is far more evenly split: 17 of 34 losses had existing AP automation, and 17 did not.

This tells a clear story: **Ottimate's healthcare go-to-market is most effective when selling into greenfield accounts** — organizations that have never automated AP and are evaluating it for the first time. These prospects are comparing Ottimate against doing nothing, not against a competitor. The sales motion is about building urgency and justifying the investment, not winning a feature bake-off. When Ottimate does enter competitive evaluations, the results are mixed. Ottimate wins consistently against point tools with narrow capabilities (Stampli, MineralTree, Beanworks, Paymerang, PN3, Tipalti) — these competitors either don't show up, can't handle healthcare-specific complexity like multi-entity allocation, or are limited to a single function (payment-only or invoicing-only). **Ottimate's win rate against narrow-scope competitors is effectively 100%.** But Ottimate loses every time against unified procurement platforms (Coupa: 0-2) and ERP-ecosystem-native solutions (Continia: 0-1, Entrata: 0-1). These losses happen when the buying decision elevates from "best AP tool" to "fewest vendors" or "deepest ERP fit" — and when the decision-maker is a CEO or parent company, not a CFO or Controller.

### The primary competitive threat is not a named competitor — it's inaction.

Only 8 of 34 losses (24%) involved the prospect actually choosing a competitor over Ottimate. **The other 26 losses (76%) were to status quo, timing, budget, organizational change, or ERP migration sequencing.** This is the defining characteristic of a market in early adoption: prospects acknowledge the pain, see the value, but don't pull the trigger. The "aware but never adopted" cohort is almost non-existent in the data (just 1 of 49 deals), which means most healthcare organizations haven't even evaluated AP automation yet — they go from "never heard of it" to "evaluating" to "yes or not now." There's no large pool of previously-burned buyers who tried and gave up. The market is fresh.

The implication for go-to-market strategy is that **the biggest ROI on sales effort comes from pipeline quality, not competitive positioning.** Over half the losses in this dataset were structurally unwinnable — wrong ERP, mid-migration, no economic buyer, wrong product fit (procurement, not AP). Tightening qualification criteria around ERP status, economic buyer access, and whether the prospect is evaluating AP automation or procurement automation would eliminate 15–20 of these 34 losses from pipeline entirely, dramatically improving conversion rates without changing the product, the messaging, or the competitive strategy. For the greenfield accounts that do qualify, the winning motion is clear: get the CFO on the call by Stage 3, build the FTE replacement ROI model before the pricing call, and use segment-specific references (senior living CFO to senior living CFO) to compress the decision timeline. The deals that do this close in 20–47 days. The deals that don't die slowly over 6–12 months.

## Appendix: Classification Notes

**ERPs excluded from competitor classification:** Sage Intacct, Sage 50, Sage 100, Sage 300, QuickBooks (all versions), NetSuite, Microsoft Dynamics (GP, BC, NAV), Acumatica, Workday, MatrixCare, PCC/PointClickCare, Yardi, MIP, Syma. These are accounting/ERP systems, not AP automation tools, per the methodology specified.

**Document management excluded from AP automation classification:** Square Nine is used at several prospects for invoice document storage and routing but is not AP automation — it doesn't process invoices, execute approval workflows, or make payments. Its OCR module had been disabled for 5+ years at United Methodist Communities.

**DSSI classification:** DSSI is a Group Purchasing Organization (GPO) platform that handles broadline vendor invoices (food service, medical supplies) with built-in approval and payment for those specific vendors. It is classified as "existing AP automation" because it does automate a portion of AP, but it only covers broadline vendors — non-broadline invoices (utilities, services, one-off vendors) are still manual. Three prospects (Ciena, Providence, Epoch) had DSSI for broadline but manual processes for everything else.

**Anthem Memory Care / MedProcure:** These appear as two separate deals in the folder structure (under different reps) but involve the same parent organization. Both are counted as separate deals since they had separate sales processes and deal IDs. Both were lost to Coupa.

**St. Dominic Village duplicate:** This deal appears twice in the folder structure (under "Dakota Ray" and "Dakota\ Ray"). Counted once.
